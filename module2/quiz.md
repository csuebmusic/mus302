# Module 2 Checkpoint Quiz

A short quiz covering the framing reading and the five anchor tracks of Module 2. Twelve questions, multiple choice, low-stakes. Students may retake the quiz as many times as they want; highest score counts.

The structured version below is for reading. The "PASTE INTO CANVAS" block further down has each question in plain text, ready to paste one at a time when building the Canvas quiz.

## Quiz introduction (paste into the Canvas quiz description box)

Welcome to the Module 2 Checkpoint Quiz. Like the Module 1 quiz, this one is short and low-stakes. It exists to make sure the framing reading and the five anchor tracks have landed before you move on.

A few things to know before you start:

There are 12 multiple-choice questions. Plan for about 15 to 20 minutes, though you can take as long as you need. There is no time limit.

You can retake this quiz as many times as you want. Your highest score is the one that counts. If you miss a question, go back to the relevant reading or listening guide, then come back and try again.

The questions cover three things: the framing reading's vocabulary and arguments (what "foundational" means here, the roots-and-routes frame, sacred-secular crossover, the three modes of political work), specific facts and contexts from the five anchor tracks (Smith with Armstrong, Tharpe, Brown, Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five, Beyoncé), and the methodology vocabulary from Module 1 applied to the new tracks (timbre, texture, form, gesture).

You should complete the framing reading and all five listening guides before taking this quiz. The quiz is the last thing you do in Module 2, after the discussion post.

If something on the quiz seems unclear or you think a question is wrong, email me. I would rather hear about it than have you stuck.

Good luck.

## Questions (structured for reading)

### 1.

The framing reading argued that calling Black popular music "foundational" to American popular music does not mean "came first" or "the source of everything." What does it mean, in the reading's argument?

a. That the formal innovations, the recording practices, the performance traditions, and the political work of Black musicians shaped what American popular music is.
b. That Latin, Asian American, and European American traditions are derived from Black music.
c. That Black popular music is more important than the other traditions in this course.
d. That the recording industry was founded by Black musicians.

### 2.

The framing reading borrowed the phrase "roots and routes" from the scholar Paul Gilroy. What does the phrase mean, in the way the reading uses it?

a. Roots are where music came from; routes are where it traveled.
b. Roots are the African origins of the music; routes are the European routes it took to reach America.
c. Roots are rural music; routes are urban music.
d. Roots are sacred music; routes are secular music.

### 3.

The framing reading named three modes by which a song can do political work. They are:

a. Content (what a song says), form (how a song is built), and presence (who is in the room and what they sound like).
b. Lyrics, melody, and rhythm.
c. Protest, celebration, and mourning.
d. Sacred, secular, and political.

### 4.

The framing reading made an argument about the long traffic between sacred and secular music in this tradition. Which of the following best summarizes that argument?

a. Black church music has been a place of musical training since the nineteenth century, and the vocal techniques (runs, melisma, the testifying voice, call-and-response) heard in soul, R&B, and contemporary pop are church techniques first.
b. Sacred and secular music have always been kept strictly separate in Black communities.
c. Gospel music was invented after soul music, in response to soul.
d. Hip hop has no relationship to gospel or to the Black church.

### 5.

Bessie Smith's 1925 recording of "St. Louis Blues" features only three musicians in the room. They are:

a. Bessie Smith on voice, Louis Armstrong on cornet, Fred Longshaw on harmonium.
b. Bessie Smith on voice, Louis Armstrong on trumpet, Duke Ellington on piano.
c. Bessie Smith on voice, with a full New Orleans jazz band.
d. Bessie Smith on voice and guitar, Louis Armstrong on cornet, Fred Longshaw on bass.

### 6.

"St. Louis Blues" does not stay in the twelve-bar blues form throughout. The middle section of the song uses a sixteen-measure passage in a different rhythmic feel. The composer W.C. Handy described that passage as a:

a. Tango, drawn from the habanera rhythmic pattern he had encountered when he traveled to Cuba in 1900.
b. Polka, drawn from the European immigrant traditions of the Midwest.
c. Waltz, drawn from the European concert music tradition.
d. March, drawn from the brass-band tradition of New Orleans.

### 7.

Sister Rosetta Tharpe was raised in the Church of God in Christ (COGIC), the largest historically Black Pentecostal denomination in the United States. By 1944, when she recorded "Strange Things Happening Every Day," she was working in a sustained tension with COGIC and many other Black church communities because:

a. She had taken her gospel performance into nightclubs and onto recordings with secular bands, while playing the electric guitar, which the church communities saw as crossing the line between sacred and secular.
b. She had renounced her religious upbringing and refused to perform gospel music.
c. She had been excommunicated by the COGIC leadership.
d. She had publicly converted to a different denomination.

### 8.

When "Strange Things Happening Every Day" charted in 1945, it became the first gospel record to cross over to:

a. The chart that the industry would soon rename "rhythm and blues."
b. The Billboard country chart.
c. The pop chart, where it reached number one.
d. The classical music chart.

### 9.

The chorus of voices answering "I'm Black and I'm proud!" on James Brown's 1968 recording of "Say It Loud" was made up of:

a. About thirty children from Watts and Compton, brought to the Vox Studios session in Van Nuys by Brown's manager Charles Bobbitt.
b. A professional gospel choir from a Los Angeles church.
c. The other members of the James Brown band, recorded together.
d. A studio choir of session singers contracted for the session.

### 10.

"Say It Loud" does not have a verse-and-chorus structure in the way most pop songs of its era did. What is the song's form, in the listening guide's analysis?

a. A single repeating vamp, with the band looping a short rhythmic pattern for the entire three minutes while Brown's vocals organize call-and-response cycles on top.
b. A twelve-bar blues, repeated three times.
c. An extended sonata form, with three contrasting sections.
d. A through-composed song with no repeating sections.

### 11.

The listening guide for "The Message" pointed at a structural feature unusual for a 1982 hit single. The verses of the song get longer as it progresses (eight measures, then eleven, then thirteen, then sixteen, with a final verse of twenty-eight measures). The listening guide argued that this expanding-verse architecture:

a. Builds tension by deferring the return of the chorus longer and longer each time, with the form enacting the pressure that the lyrics describe.
b. Was a mistake by an inexperienced producer.
c. Is a standard verse-and-chorus structure that had been common in pop music for decades.
d. Was added to make the song long enough to fit on a twelve-inch single.

### 12.

The listening guide for Beyoncé's "Formation" identified a specific moment at 0:55 of the recording where the song's character changes. What happens at that moment?

a. The marching-band horns enter for the first time, layering a New Orleans second-line stomp on top of the Atlanta trap beat that has carried the song until then.
b. Beyoncé switches from singing to rapping for the rest of the song.
c. The Messy Mya sample plays for the first time.
d. The drums drop out of the mix entirely.
