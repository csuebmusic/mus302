# Module 3 Discussion: Pick a Reflective Question

This is the discussion thread for Module 3. The work happens in two stages: an initial post in which you pick one of the five reflective questions at the end of the Module 3 listening guides and respond to it, and two responses to classmates' posts that Canvas will assign you automatically after the initial-post deadline.

## How it works

Each of the five Module 3 listening guides ends with a reflective question. The questions are:

- **Tito Puente, "Oye Como Va" (1962)**: Listen to the 1962 recording on its own terms before hearing the Santana version. Pick one moment and argue what the recording is doing that a 1971 rock arrangement might preserve, alter, or lose. The question is not whether one version is better; it is what the song is, in its 1962 form, that a different ensemble in a different city eight years later would have to make decisions about.
- **Joe Bataan, "Gypsy Woman" (1967)**: Bataan was Filipino and African American, raised inside Spanish Harlem's Puerto Rican world. Pick one moment in the recording and argue what the song is doing with cultural identity. What does this recording suggest about how the categories the course uses (Black, Latin, Asian American, white working-class) actually work in the lives and music of the people who made the categories real?
- **Santana, "Oye Como Va" (1970)**: The Santana arrangement keeps the song's vamp form intact while changing almost every other surface. Pick one moment and argue what the form makes possible. What kind of cultural work is the Santana arrangement doing that the Puente arrangement is not, and what stays constant across both versions because the form lets it?
- **Selena, "Bidi Bidi Bom Bom" (1994)**: The song's lyric is entirely in Spanish, its title is in no language at all, and its musical surface is a Colombian rhythm processed through a Texas border genre with American pop production. Pick one moment and argue what this Tejano cumbia synthesis is doing. What does it mean for a Mexican-American teenager from Corpus Christi to be the most popular Latin music star in the United States in 1994 with a song built on an onomatopoeia that crosses any language?
- **Tego Calderón, "Pa' Que Retozen" (2002)**: The track is sparser than any other recording in this module and is also the most-streamed by an enormous margin. What changed between 1962 and 2002 in the relationship between texture and reach, and what does the four-decade arc from a twenty-piece Cuban orchestra to a one-rapper-and-a-beat track tell you about the changing politics of who gets to make Latin popular music and how it gets to circulate?

Pick one. Read the listening guide for the track if you have not already, then write your post in response to that track's reflective question. The full questions live in the listening guides themselves, with more context than the summaries above.

## What a strong post looks like

A strong post does four things:

- It picks one specific track and engages with that track's reflective question directly. Quoting back part of the question is fine; your job is to answer it.
- It rests on at least one specific moment in the recording. Timestamps are great. So is a precise description of what is happening in the moment.
- It uses the vocabulary the readings have given you. Timbre, texture, form, gesture; content, form, presence as the three modes of political work; the dialogue between Latin diasporic music and African American music that runs through the framing reading. You do not have to use all of these. You should use the ones that fit your argument.
- It makes a claim someone could disagree with. The reflective questions are designed to invite a position, not a summary.

Two to four paragraphs is the right length. Longer is fine if you have more to say. Shorter is fine if you say it well.

## Responding to classmates

After the initial-post deadline, Canvas will assign you two classmates' posts to respond to. You will not pick them yourself; Canvas's peer-review tool handles assignment automatically. This means you will likely be reading posts on tracks you did not pick, which is part of the design: the thread spreads coverage across all five tracks rather than concentrating on the most popular questions.

Good responses do one or more of these things:

- Engage with the specific moment your classmate pointed to. If you heard it differently, say so. "I heard that moment doing something else, here is what" is welcome.
- Add to the argument by pointing at a moment in the same track that fits or complicates what your classmate said.
- Connect the post to a moment in another Module 3 track or in earlier modules. Cross-track connections are part of the work, especially given the dialogues this module is designed to make audible.
- Ask a question that pushes the argument further. Not "great post!" but something more like: "you said Carlos Santana's guitar solo on 'Oye Como Va' is doing the work of translation; do you think the percussion section is doing translation work too, or is it carrying the song's continuity with Puente's version?"

## Logistics

- Initial post: due by Sunday, June 21 (the Module 3 deadline)
- Two assigned responses: due by Sunday, July 5 (the gap is wider here than in other modules because the project proposal and preliminary bibliography are due in between, on Sunday, June 28)
- Length: two to four paragraphs for the initial post, a paragraph or two for each response
- AI use: allowed with disclosure, per the generative AI policy in the syllabus. If you used AI for this post, end your post with a one-sentence acknowledgment ("I used [tool] to [purpose].")

## Outcomes addressed

This discussion supports SLO 2 (communicating critical thoughts and judgments about the creative work of different artists), SLO 3 (cultural connections), and SLO 5 (identifying and discussing forms and influences within and across traditions). It also supports the GE-UD-3 outcome on applying the methodologies of the arts and humanities, and the Diversity Overlay outcomes on describing the histories of cultural groups and on the intersection of social identities (the Bataan track in particular foregrounds intersection across cultural categories).
