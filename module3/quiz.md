# Module 3 Checkpoint Quiz

A short quiz covering the framing reading and the five anchor tracks of Module 3. Twelve questions, multiple choice, low-stakes. Students may retake the quiz as many times as they want; highest score counts.

The structured version below is for reading. The "PASTE INTO CANVAS" block further down has each question in plain text, ready to paste one at a time when building the Canvas quiz.

## Quiz introduction (paste into the Canvas quiz description box)

Welcome to the Module 3 Checkpoint Quiz. Like the Module 1 and Module 2 quizzes, this one is short and low-stakes. It exists to make sure the framing reading and the five anchor tracks have landed before you move on.

A few things to know before you start:

There are 12 multiple-choice questions. Plan for about 15 to 20 minutes, though you can take as long as you need. There is no time limit.

You can retake this quiz as many times as you want. Your highest score is the one that counts. If you miss a question, go back to the relevant reading or listening guide, then come back and try again.

The questions cover three things: the framing reading's vocabulary and arguments (what "Latin diasporic" means here, the Caribbean-and-Mexican substrate, the migrations that built the communities, the long dialogue with African American music), specific facts and contexts from the five anchor tracks (Puente, Bataan, Santana, Selena, Tego Calderón), and the methodology vocabulary from Module 1 applied to the new tracks (timbre, texture, form, gesture, plus the rhythmic vocabulary you have been building).

You should complete the framing reading and all five listening guides before taking this quiz. The quiz is the last thing you do in Module 3, after the discussion post.

If something on the quiz seems unclear or you think a question is wrong, email me. I would rather hear about it than have you stuck.

Good luck.

## Questions (structured for reading)

### 1.

The framing reading argued that "Latin" as a category for music in the United States is a "category of convenience." What did the reading mean by that?

a. The communities the term covers do not share a single national origin, migration history, relationship to the United States, or musical tradition; the term is useful but flattens distinctions the music itself does not.
b. The term was invented by record labels in the 1970s to market a new product category.
c. The term applies only to music sung in Spanish, with no other criteria.
d. The term covers only music from the Caribbean.

### 2.

The framing reading borrowed and modified the "roots and routes" formulation it had used in Module 2. The Module 3 version, captured in the reading's title "Many Roots, Shared Routes," describes:

a. A family of traditions whose origins are plural (Afro-Cuban, Afro-Puerto Rican, mestizo Mexican, and others), but whose paths into and through the United States ran through overlapping cities, neighborhoods, labels, and audiences.
b. A single musical tradition that has spread across many cities.
c. The migration routes Latin musicians took back to their home countries.
d. The trade routes that brought Spanish colonizers to the Americas.

### 3.

The framing reading argued that the diasporic relationship for Mexican-American musicians in Texas and California runs differently from that of Cuban or Puerto Rican musicians. The argument was:

a. The community did not move; the border moved. Texas and California were Mexican territory until the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo in 1848 transferred them to the United States, so Mexican-descent communities in San Antonio, Los Angeles, and the Central Valley have continuous histories that predate the border.
b. Mexican-American musicians have no diasporic relationship to Mexico because they live in the United States.
c. Mexican migration to the United States only began in the 1980s.
d. Mexican-American musicians return to Mexico every year, unlike Cuban or Puerto Rican musicians.

### 4.

Conjunto, the foundational South Texas Mexican-American ensemble that is the direct ancestor of Tejano, is built around two specific instruments. They are:

a. Accordion and bajo sexto, which came out of the meeting of Mexican folk song with the polkas and waltzes of late-nineteenth-century German and Czech immigrants who settled in central Texas and northern Mexico.
b. Guitar and trumpet, drawn from the mariachi tradition.
c. Marimba and clarinet, drawn from the Mexican concert music tradition.
d. Piano and violin, drawn from the European classical music tradition.

### 5.

Tito Puente's 1962 recording of "Oye Como Va" was made at the Hotel Riverside Plaza Ballroom in Manhattan with an orchestra of roughly twenty musicians. Among them was Johnny Pacheco, who played the lead flute on the recording. Pacheco's significance beyond this session is that he was:

a. A Dominican flutist who would three years later co-found Fania Records and rename the New York Cuban-based dance idiom "salsa."
b. The leader of his own competing Cuban-American orchestra at the Palladium.
c. A trombone-playing arranger who later joined Eddie Palmieri's band.
d. A Mexican-American musician who introduced Puente to Carlos Santana.

### 6.

The Track 1 listening guide argued that "Oye Como Va" 's central piano figure (a block-chord tumbao) is built on an asymmetrical pattern across the bar, with the chord hits falling at positions that do not line up evenly with the four-beat downbeat grid. The pedagogical point of attending to this asymmetry is that:

a. The mismatch between the dancer's grid and the piano's grid is the song's central pleasure, and is closely related to what salsa pianists call a montuno and what funk drummers call the pocket.
b. The piano part is a mistake on the part of the pianist.
c. The asymmetry only happens on the recording and not in live performance.
d. The asymmetry is unique to this song and does not appear elsewhere in Latin music.

### 7.

Joe Bataan was born in Spanish Harlem in 1942 and is one of the central figures of the mid-1960s Latin soul and boogaloo scene. His parentage and upbringing were:

a. A Filipino father from Manila and an African American mother from Newport News, Virginia, raised in a mostly Puerto Rican neighborhood where he learned Spanish on the street from his playmates.
b. Two Puerto Rican parents who arrived in New York in the postwar Great Migration.
c. A Cuban exile father and a Mexican-American mother who met in Miami before moving to New York.
d. Two Filipino parents who emigrated from Manila after World War II.

### 8.

The Track 2 listening guide argued that "Gypsy Woman" 's form is built on contrast between two distinct moods that take turns. They are:

a. Pop-soul verse passages where Bataan sings the Mayfield-derived English lyric over a slower-feeling soul groove, alternating with boogaloo break passages where the chorus shouts in Spanish, the handclaps come in on the offbeat, and the trombones push the band to double time.
b. A slow ballad section followed by a fast dance section.
c. A verse-chorus-bridge structure repeated three times.
d. A single vamp held for the entire track.

### 9.

Carlos Santana's 1970 recording of "Oye Como Va" keeps the song's underlying form intact (the same A Dorian tonality, the same Am7-D9 vamp, the same essentially cha-cha-chá pulse) but replaces the New York charanga voicings with a different instrumental palette. The Track 3 listening guide argued that the form makes this transmission possible because:

a. A vamp-based form is open enough to host any soloists, so each band can put its own voices in the hot seat without changing the song's underlying skeleton.
b. The form is so simple that any band can play it without preparation.
c. The form is unique to Santana's arrangement and was not present in the Puente original.
d. Latin music has no fixed forms, so the song could be remade in any way.

### 10.

The Track 4 listening guide described "Bidi Bidi Bom Bom" as a Tejano cumbia recording. The Tejano cumbia of the 1980s and 1990s was, in the listening guide's argument, a synthesis of:

a. The Colombian cumbia rhythm, fused with Tejano accordion and bajo sexto, mainstream pop production, synthesizers, electric guitars, and contemporary R&B and dancehall reggae.
b. Mexican mariachi music and American country music.
c. Cuban son and Puerto Rican salsa.
d. Polka and waltz, with no other influences.

### 11.

The Track 4 listening guide argued that the title phrase "bidi bidi bom bom" is doing two specific things at once. They are:

a. Reaching for the same universal-readability that English-language nonsense pop syllables ("doo-wop," "shoop shoop") have always done in American pop, and grounding the song in the embodied physical fact of a heartbeat, which is the same in any language.
b. Translating directly from a Spanish phrase about heartbreak.
c. Using a sound effect borrowed from Mexican folk music.
d. Quoting an earlier Selena single's hook.

### 12.

Tego Calderón's 2002 "Pa' Que Retozen" is built on the dembow riddim, the rhythmic skeleton of essentially all reggaeton. The dembow riddim was originally:

a. Built by the producer Bobby "Digital" Dixon for Shabba Ranks's 1990 dancehall single "Dem Bow," and adapted by Panamanian and Puerto Rican producers ever since.
b. Created by Tego Calderón specifically for "Pa' Que Retozen" in 2002.
c. A Cuban son rhythm that crossed to Puerto Rico in the 1970s.
d. An American hip hop pattern from the early 1980s.
