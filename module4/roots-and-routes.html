Module 4: Asian American Traditions · Reading

Framing Reading A Category That Had to Be Invented

This module covers a family of traditions, not a single one. Chinese American and Japanese American jazz on the West Coast; Filipino American jazz on the Pacific and Filipino American R&B and pop in the postwar United States; the Asian American Movement's folk and protest music of the early 1970s; the Asian American Jazz movement of the 1980s; the Bay Area Filipino American mobile DJ scene that became the cradle of turntablism; Tongan American and other Pacific Islander pop; the indie rock and contemporary work that has pushed Asian American voices into the center of American popular music in the 2010s. The communities who made this music are distinct. Their migration histories, their relationships to American empire, their experiences of and internment and the postwar refugee waves all differ. They share a few important things: a long history of producing American popular music while being read by mainstream culture as either invisible or foreign; a sustained dialogue with African American music, including direct political solidarity with Black liberation movements; and the specific experience of being grouped together under a category that, for most of US history, did not exist.

Modules 2 and 3 traced histories of communities that already had names. Black music in the United States, before any module read about it, was already understood by Americans as Black music; Latin music as Latin music. Module 4 covers a category that the people inside it had to invent. The phrase "Asian American" was coined in May 1968 by two graduate students at UC Berkeley, just over the hill from where you are reading this, as a deliberate political alternative to the older racial term "Oriental." Before then, the communities this module covers were thought of separately, by themselves and by everyone else: Chinese, Japanese, Filipino, Korean, sometimes , sometimes Pacific Islander, often "Oriental." The first record album recorded under the new banner of "Asian American" came five years later, in 1973: A Grain of Sand: Music for the Struggle by Asians in America, the album the first track of this module is drawn from. Music was central to giving the category its early substance.

That historical sequence (the term first, then the music that learned to use it) shapes how this reading is structured. The reading walks through eight things you need before the listening guides: the conceptual move that the term "Asian American" makes; the long pre-history of Asian musicians in the United States before 1965, when there was no such category; the 1965 Immigration and Nationality Act and what it did to the population the music came out of; the Asian American Movement of 1968-1973 that built the category and recorded its first album; the Asian American Jazz movement of the 1980s and the Vincent Chin murder that catalyzed it; the Tongan American family band that landed in the pop Top 10 inside the Minneapolis sound; the Bay Area Filipino American mobile DJ scene that became the cradle of and turntablism; and the indie rock track that closes the module by staging a young Asian American woman's relationship to a "great big American flag" of a boy. The reading ends with a short, honest section about what an eight-section framing and five anchor tracks have to leave out.

What "Asian American" means here

The phrase "Asian American" looks like a straightforward descriptive category, parallel to "African American" or "Latin American." It is not. It was coined for a specific political purpose at a specific moment, and understanding the moment is part of understanding the music.

In May 1968, Yuji Ichioka and Emma Gee, two graduate students at UC Berkeley, called a meeting at their apartment at 2005 Hearst Avenue. Ichioka was a Japanese American historian who had been incarcerated as a child at the Topaz in Utah; Gee was a Chinese American activist and writer. Both had been participating in anti-Vietnam War, , and civil rights demonstrations and had noticed the same thing. Asian Americans were present at these protests in real numbers, but they had no shared identity to mobilize around, no banner to march under. They tended to identify ethnically (as Chinese, Japanese, Filipino) and in those ethnic groupings their numbers were small. Ichioka's hypothesis was that a panethnic identity, combining the ethnic groups under one umbrella, would give Asian Americans political visibility they had lacked. The meeting at 2005 Hearst Avenue founded the Asian American Political Alliance (AAPA), and the name they chose for their group was, as far as anyone has established, the first public use of the phrase "Asian American."

The choice was deliberate. The alternative was "Oriental," a term inherited from European colonial discourse that lumped Asian peoples together as exotic, foreign, and feminine, and that white Americans had used for a century without consultation. Richard Aoki, a Japanese American AAPA founder who was also a Black Panther Party field marshal, put the rejection bluntly: "Oriental was a rug that everyone steps on, so we ain't no Orientals." Replacing "Oriental" with "Asian American" was a refusal of the old framing and a claim to the American part of the identity. It also performed a unification. Chinese Americans and Japanese Americans had been on opposite sides of the Pacific War a generation earlier; Filipinos and Koreans had distinct colonial relationships to Japan; South Asian Americans had a different immigration history from East Asian Americans. The new term insisted that, in the American context, the racial coding these communities encountered (and the political possibilities they could mobilize together) was shared.

The word for what the new term did is panethnic. A identity is one that gathers multiple ethnic groups into a shared political category, on the grounds that they face common conditions and can do common political work. "Latino" and "Hispanic" function similarly, gathering communities of Mexican, Puerto Rican, Cuban, Dominican, and other origins under a shared category that has political and demographic uses while flattening real distinctions. "Asian American" works the same way. The communities it gathers do not share a homeland, a language, a religion, or a single migration history. They do, in the United States, share a racial coding (read as foreign, read as a single bloc, read as the "Yellow Peril" of nineteenth-century anti-Chinese propaganda or the "Asian model minority" of twentieth-century white-supremacist sociology), and they share a long history of legal exclusion (the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882, the Asian Exclusion Act of 1924, Japanese American internment under Executive Order 9066, the post-1975 refugee resettlement of Vietnamese, Cambodian, and Lao communities). The panethnic move was a way of refusing to be divided in the face of those conditions.

It is worth pausing on one further complication, because this module's third track will hinge on it. The federal government's racial classification, since the 2000 census, has used the category "Asian or Pacific Islander" (AAPI), folding Pacific Islander communities (Native Hawaiian, Samoan, Tongan, Chamorro, and others) into a single bureaucratic grouping with Asian Americans. Many Pacific Islander writers and organizers have pushed back on this folding, because Pacific Islander histories are different in important ways. Pacific Islander communities are to lands that the United States colonized (Hawaii annexed in 1898, Samoa partitioned in 1899, Guam acquired in 1898), which makes the relationship to the United States categorically different from voluntary or forced immigration. The 1990 separation of "Pacific Islander" as its own census category in some federal contexts, and the 2000 standardization of "AAPI" as a combined category, both reflect this contested grouping. This module's third track is by The Jets, a Tongan American family band, and the question of whether Pacific Islander histories belong inside an Asian American module is a real one. The course's answer is to include them while flagging the complication, which is what the federal AAPI category does and what Pacific Islander writers continue to argue about.

So when this module uses the phrase "Asian American" to name the territory it covers, the phrase is doing several things at once. It is a recent political invention. It folds together communities whose individual histories in the United States are long and substantially distinct. It includes (with reasonable disagreement) Pacific Islander communities. It is the term the people who made the music chose, often deliberately, to do their political and creative work under, and the music that resulted is part of how the term came to mean something. The first track of this module is the moment that meaning-making started.

The long pre-history

Asian musicians have been making American popular music since the late nineteenth century, more than seventy years before there was an "Asian American" category to name what they were doing. Three threads are worth pulling out, because each shows up in the music covered in this module's anchor tracks.

The first thread is the Filipino musician on the Pacific. The United States annexed the Philippines in 1898, at the end of the Spanish-American War, and held the islands as a colony until 1946. Spanish colonial musical practices (the rondalla, the choral tradition, the European church music inherited from Spain's three-and-a-half-century occupation) were already deep in Philippine musical life when the Americans arrived. American occupation added , foxtrot, military band music, vaudeville, and, by the late 1910s, jazz. Filipino musicians learned the new repertoire fast and exported it back. By the 1920s, Filipino musicians were the dominant non-white labor force in jazz orchestras in hotels and ocean liners across Asia: Manila, Hong Kong, Shanghai, Kobe, Yokohama, Calcutta, Singapore. Frederick Schenker, a music historian who has written about this scene, calls Filipino musicians "the unsung Pacific carriers of jazz." Some Japanese musicians of the period have said the first time they heard a musician improvise (rather than play strictly from sheet music) it was a Filipino doing it. This is a tradition older than the term "Asian American," older than most of the recorded history of jazz in the United States, and it is the substrate on which Filipino American musical practice in California built. When this module's fourth track gets to the Bay Area Filipino American DJ scene of the 1980s and 1990s, the line back to Filipino musicians on Pacific ocean liners in the 1920s is a continuous one.

The second thread is the Chinese American and Japanese American jazz scene of the prewar West Coast. By the 1930s, San Francisco's Chinatown had at least twelve Chinese cabarets running, the most famous being Charlie Low's Forbidden City, which opened in 1938 and was sometimes called the "Cotton Club of the West" in implicit comparison to the famous Black-staffed-and-white-patron-only Harlem nightclub. Chinese, Japanese, Filipino, and Korean dancers and musicians worked at the Forbidden City, including the Chinese American singer Larry Ching, sometimes billed as "the Chinese Frank Sinatra," and Katy de la Cruz, a Filipina American sometimes billed as the "Queen of Filipino Jazz." Across town and across the bay, second-generation Japanese Americans (the , US-born children of immigrants from Japan) formed jazz orchestras with names like the Cathayans, the Japanese Sandmen, and the Sho Tokyans. In Los Angeles, an all-Japanese American brass band called the LA Mikado Band had been performing since 1914. None of this material was recorded under the banner of "Asian American" because no such banner existed; it was recorded as jazz, period, performed by people whose racial coding was visible at the venue and audible in the press but not central to the music's category.

The third thread is what happened to the Japanese American jazz scene in 1942. On February 19, 1942, ten weeks after Pearl Harbor, President Franklin Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066, which authorized the forced removal and incarceration of approximately 120,000 people of Japanese ancestry living on the West Coast, two-thirds of them US citizens, into ten concentration camps run by the War Relocation Authority. (The official US government term was "internment," and we will use that term in this reading because that is the term you will encounter in most sources, while noting that scholars and survivors increasingly use "incarceration" because "internment" implies a wartime detention of foreign nationals, which most of the people incarcerated were not.) Japanese American musical life did not stop in the camps. It continued, in chamber ensembles and dance bands and church choirs, on improvised stages in tar-paper barracks. After the war, the Nisei and the next generation, the , came out of the camps and back into a country that had stripped their families of homes, businesses, and savings. The musicians among them rebuilt their careers; some continued in jazz (Paul Togawa, the drummer who had been a teenage internee, became a working Los Angeles jazz drummer in the 1950s and acted in Rodgers and Hammerstein's Flower Drum Song); others moved into mainstream entertainment (Pat Suzuki, a second-generation Japanese American who had been incarcerated as a child, was discovered in a Seattle nightclub in the late 1950s by Bing Crosby and went on to a Broadway run in the 1958 production of Flower Drum Song and a string of jazz-standard albums). Two decades before the term "Asian American" existed, Suzuki was the most successful Asian American musician working in mainstream American entertainment, and her career was shaped end-to-end by the conditions internment had created.

Three threads, three communities, three different relationships to American empire and American music. They are pre-history in the sense that the people doing the work did not yet have a shared name for what they were doing. They are not pre-history in any other sense. The musical practices and the musicians' children and the institutional infrastructure (Forbidden City, the Issei brass bands, the Filipino musicians' union locals on the West Coast) all carried forward into what came next. When the Movement generation of 1968-1973 started using the new term "Asian American" to talk about the music they wanted to make, they were not starting from nothing. They were renaming a long history.

1965 and what changed

The legal-historical context that made the music covered in this module possible is concentrated, by accident or by design, around two single years: 1965 and 1968. The 1965 year is about who was allowed to be in the country. The 1968 year is about what the people in the country called themselves. Take them in order.

For most of US history, the laws governing who could enter the country and who could become a citizen were explicitly racial. Asian people, with rare exceptions, could not. The first restriction was the of 1882, which barred most Chinese laborers from entering the country and made existing Chinese residents ineligible for naturalization. The 1907 Gentlemen's Agreement informally restricted Japanese immigration by diplomatic understanding. The 1917 Immigration Act created an "Asiatic Barred Zone" covering most of South and Southeast Asia. The (also called the Johnson-Reed Act, also called the Asian Exclusion Act) formalized a national-origin quota system that effectively closed off most Asian immigration outside the Philippines, which was then a US colony. The 1934 closed even that loophole, granting eventual Philippine independence and reducing Filipino immigration to 50 people per year. in 1942 forced approximately 120,000 Japanese Americans into incarceration camps. The 1943 Magnuson Act repealed Chinese Exclusion (with a token quota of 105 Chinese immigrants per year). The 1946 Luce-Celler Act doubled the Filipino quota to 100 per year and finally allowed Filipinos and South Asians to naturalize. The 1952 McCarran-Walter Act removed the racial bars on naturalization but kept the national-origin quotas in place. From 1882 to 1965, in other words, US immigration law treated Asian people as a problem to be managed by exclusion. Eighty-three years of legal restriction, each piece of which singled out a specific Asian community for specific barriers, makes the Asian American population that existed in the United States in 1965 a population shaped by exclusion to a degree that is hard to overstate.

On October 3, 1965, President Lyndon Johnson signed the (also called the Hart-Celler Act, after its sponsors Senator Philip Hart of Michigan and Representative Emanuel Celler of Brooklyn) at a ceremony at the foot of the Statue of Liberty. The new law removed the national-origin quotas. Each country was given the same annual cap of 20,000. The law set up a preference system: 75 percent of admissions for family reunification, 20 percent for employment-based admission with a strong tilt toward "highly skilled" professionals, 5 percent for refugee status. Johnson's signing remarks called the old quotas "a very deep and painful flaw in the fabric of American justice." Most of the legislators voting for the bill, and most of the press covering its passage, expected it to be a symbolic correction with little demographic consequence; the assumption was that European immigrants would continue to dominate, just without the racial framework.

That was wrong. In the years immediately after 1965, the Asian American population in the United States, which had been less than one percent of the total US population, began to grow rapidly. By the 2010s it was almost six percent. The composition of that population changed in three important ways. First, the existing Asian American communities (largely Chinese, Japanese, and Filipino) doubled, then doubled again, as the family reunification preference let community members bring relatives in. Second, new communities arrived in significant numbers for the first time: Korean Americans, Indian Americans, Pakistani Americans, Bangladeshi Americans. The Korean American population in particular grew almost from scratch in the 1970s and 1980s. Third, the 1975 Indochina Migration and Refugee Assistance Act, passed in the immediate aftermath of the fall of Saigon, brought more than 300,000 refugees from Vietnam, Cambodia, and Laos to the United States between 1975 and 1979, and additional waves in the 1980s. The Vietnamese, Cambodian, Lao, and Hmong American communities that exist in the United States today are largely the result of that displacement and the wars (the Vietnam War, the Khmer Rouge genocide, the Secret War in Laos) that drove it. The 1965 Act and its 1975 refugee follow-on transformed the demographic that the term "Asian American" would have to describe.

The musicians on this module's anchor tracks come from this transformation. The trio who recorded A Grain of Sand in 1973 are second- and third-generation Japanese American and Chinese American children of pre-1965 immigrants; their work was made possible by the institutions the Movement built in the early 1970s. Jon Jang, on the second track, is a US-born child of Chinese immigrants. The Wolfgramm family of The Jets came from Tonga in the post-1965 wave; Maikeli and Vela Wolfgramm immigrated and raised their seventeen children in Salt Lake City and Minneapolis. The Filipino American DJs of the Bay Area scene on Track 4 are predominantly children and grandchildren of post-1965 Filipino immigrants whose families came to Daly City and Vallejo and Union City through the family-reunification clause and the medical and educational professions the new law favored. Mitski, on Track 5, is the Japanese-and-American daughter of an internationally mobile family whose musical career happens entirely in the post-1965 American context. The 1965 Act is not visible in any single track's lyrics or sound, but it is the precondition for almost every musician this module covers.

The Movement and A Grain of Sand

The 1968 founding of the Asian American Political Alliance was one moment in a wider mobilization that historians now call the Asian American Movement. The Movement ran roughly from 1968 to the mid-1970s, drew on the Black Power and Movements as direct models, and produced both the panethnic political identity the module's first section described and the institutional infrastructure that gave that identity substance. Three pieces of that infrastructure matter here.

The first is academic. The (TWLF) strike at San Francisco State College, which began on November 6, 1968, and lasted five months, was a coalition strike led by the Black Student Union with Asian American (AAPA's SF State chapter, the Filipino-American Collegiate Endeavor, and the Intercollegiate Chinese for Social Action), Latino, and Native American student groups. The strike's settlement on March 20, 1969 established the first College of Ethnic Studies in the United States. UC Berkeley's parallel TWLF strike began on January 22, 1969 and ended with UC President Charles Hitch authorizing the establishment of the UC Berkeley Ethnic Studies Department on March 7, 1969. The first Asian American Studies courses in the United States were taught in those new programs in 1969 and 1970, with Yuji Ichioka teaching the first one at UCLA. The intellectual infrastructure that gave the term "Asian American" its academic meaning was built in those two strikes within a few months of each other in the Bay Area.

The second piece of infrastructure is community-based. In New York, an organization called Basement Workshop emerged in 1970, in a tenement basement at 54 Elizabeth Street in Manhattan's Chinatown. Basement Workshop began as an outgrowth of the 1969 Chinatown Study, an urban planning report by Columbia graduate students on the conditions in NYC's Chinatown, and grew into the first pan-Asian American political and arts organization on the East Coast. Its members were urban planners, community organizers, and artists. It ran English-language and citizenship classes, organized health fairs (one of which grew into the Charles B. Wang Community Health Center), published a quarterly magazine called Bridge, and incubated several arts and community organizations that still exist (the Museum of Chinese in America among them). In 1972, Basement Workshop published Yellow Pearl, a 12-inch-record-sized box of fifty-four loose folios printed on yellow paper, containing poetry, visual art, and song lyrics by more than thirty Asian American artists. The title plays off "Yellow Peril," the racist slogan from the late nineteenth-century anti-Chinese exclusion campaigns. Yellow Pearl included sheet music for songs by Chris Iijima and Nobuko Miyamoto, who were among the regulars in the Basement Workshop's orbit.

The third piece of infrastructure is musical. Iijima and Miyamoto met as activists in the New York Asian American Movement scene at the start of the 1970s. Iijima was the son of two Japanese American Movement organizers (his parents helped found Asian Americans for Action, the first Asian American civil rights organization on the East Coast). Miyamoto was a Sansei (third-generation) Japanese American from Los Angeles who had been incarcerated as a small child at the Manzanar camp and who had come to New York as a Broadway dancer (she had been in the original cast of West Side Story) before turning toward activism. The two of them performed together at Movement events around the city, often with Charlie Chin, a Chinese American multi-instrumentalist who had played in folk and rock bands in the 1960s. They sometimes called themselves Yellow Pearl, after the Basement Workshop publication. They wrote songs about Asian American identity, the Vietnam War, the labor history of the manong generation of Filipino farmworkers, and solidarity with Black, Latino, and Native liberation struggles. They performed at Madison Square Garden for Puerto Rican Liberation Day in front of an estimated twenty thousand people. In 1972 they appeared on The Mike Douglas Show during the week John Lennon and Yoko Ono co-hosted; Iijima sang one of their songs on national television.

In 1972, after a concert at the Ash Grove in Los Angeles, the trio met Barbara Dane. Dane was a singer and a longtime Movement musician (she had performed with Louis Armstrong and was once told by Bob Dylan's manager Albert Grossman to call him back when she "got her priorities straight"). She and her husband Irwin Silber had founded a record label called Paredon Records in 1969-1970, dedicated to releasing music from political movements around the world. Paredon's catalog included albums from Cuba, Vietnam, Angola, Palestine, Ireland, Chile, and Puerto Rico. Dane invited the trio to record an album for the label. They went into A-1 Sound Studios in New York and recorded, in two and a half days, the twelve songs that became A Grain of Sand: Music for the Struggle by Asians in America. Paredon released the album in late 1973. The cover art was by Arlon Huang and Karl Matsuda, two Basement Workshop visual artists. The personnel list inside the gatefold credits Iijima and Chin on acoustic guitar, Chin on bass and flute, Miyamoto on maracas, the percussionist Attalah on conga drums, and the engineer Jonathan Thayer. Mutulu Shakur, a Black Panther and Republic of New Afrika activist, contributed backing vocals on several tracks; his stepson Tupac Shakur reportedly sang along to the record as a child.

A Grain of Sand was the first record album to be released under the banner of "Asian American" music. The phrase was barely five years old. The trio had no commercial precedent for what they were trying to do; their first recording before A Grain of Sand was actually a 45 done with the Puerto Rican label Discos Coqui after they had toured with the Puerto Rican duo Pepe y Flora, which suggests the kind of cross-tradition Movement networks the album came out of. The album sold modestly. Paredon was a small label whose catalog was distributed through left-wing bookstores and movement organizations rather than commercial radio; A Grain of Sand never approached a chart and was largely unknown outside Movement circles. Its later canonization (Smithsonian Folkways acquired the Paredon catalog in 1991, reissued the album on CD in 2004, and treats it now as the founding document of Asian American music) happened decades after the recording itself. At the time, the album was an artifact of a movement, not a piece of commercial pop.

This module's first track, "We Are the Children," is the second song on the album. The song's lyric names what the trio was claiming for themselves and the new identity: "we are the children of the migrant worker, we are the offspring of the concentration camp, sons and daughters of the railroad builder, who leave their stamp on Amerika." (The "k" is theirs; it was a common Movement-era spelling that flagged a critical relationship to the country's history.) The chorus pulls forward to the present: "we are a part of the third world people, who will leave our stamp on Amerika." The song does several things at once: it claims a specific Asian American history (the migrant worker, the incarcerated, the Chinese laborers who built the western half of the transcontinental railroad), it places that history inside a wider Third World political community, and it announces a generation that will not stay invisible. The listening guide for the track will go into the song's musical detail. What is worth holding onto from the framing reading is that the song is less an aesthetic object than a declaration: this is what Asian American music sounds like when you have just decided that there is such a thing as Asian American music.

The Asian American Jazz movement and Vincent Chin

By the late 1970s, the Bay Area Asian American Movement had moved past its first wave of consciousness-raising into the long political work of institutional building. The eviction of the elderly Filipino tenants of the on Kearny Street in San Francisco's Manilatown, on August 4, 1977, was the moment that work crystallized around. The I-Hotel had been the last block of Manilatown, a ten-block Filipino American neighborhood that had been steadily eaten by Financial District redevelopment since the 1960s. A nine-year tenant-led campaign had tried to save it. On the night of August 4, 1977, more than three hundred sheriff's deputies and police officers, mostly on horseback, broke through a three-thousand-person human barricade and forcibly removed the remaining tenants, most of them aging Filipino generation laborers. The eviction was the largest civil-disobedience event in Asian American history to that date and one of the most visible demonstrations of the displacement that postwar urban redevelopment had inflicted on Asian American communities. Among the organizations that had been working out of the I-Hotel basement in the years before the eviction were the Asian Community Center, the Chinese Progressive Association, Everybody's Bookstore (the first Asian American bookstore in the country), and the , a multidisciplinary arts collective founded in 1972.

Kearny Street Workshop relocated after the eviction. In 1981, two of its members, the writer George Leong and the bookseller Paul Yamazaki, founded the Asian American Jazz Festival in San Francisco. The first festival's lineup included United Front (a multiethnic Bay Area jazz collective), the Russel Baba and Eddie Moore duo, Randy Senzaki and Rudy Tenio, Mark Izu's trio, the Filipino American bandleader Pat Salavar's Mindinao Band, and a quartet led by the Japanese American drummer Kenny Endo. The festival ran continuously for twenty-five years, until 2006, and became the longest-running jazz festival in San Francisco history. , a Sansei Japanese American bassist born in Vallejo who studied gagaku (Japanese imperial court music) for twenty-six years with Imperial Household master Togi Suenobu, took over as artistic director in the mid-1980s and held the role through 2000. (Izu died in January 2025; his archive of more than 130 archival recordings of festival performances is held by the San Francisco Museum of Performance and Design and the National Japanese American Historical Society.)

The musicians the festival gathered, alongside parallel scenes in New York and Chicago, came to be called the Asian American Jazz movement. Its core figures included Izu, the Chinese American pianist , the Chinese American saxophonist Francis Wong, the Japanese American drummer and ethnomusicologist Anthony Brown, the Japanese American pianist Glenn Horiuchi, the Korean American kotoist Miya Masaoka, and the Asian American baritone saxophonist (born Fred Wei-Han Houn). The Indian American pianist Vijay Iyer joined the orbit in the 1990s. The movement's distinguishing musical move was to combine the formal language of post-1960s African American jazz (free improvisation, modal and post-bop harmony, the long-form suite tradition that runs from Ellington and Mingus through Coltrane and the AACM) with traditional Asian musical materials and instruments: gagaku and shō (Izu, Brown), Chinese folk melody and sheng (Jang), the drum tradition (Horiuchi, Endo), the Korean changgu and koto (Masaoka), Chinese opera (Ho). The ensembles were almost always multiethnic, often with African American collaborators (the trumpeter Eddie Moore, the saxophonist James Newton, the saxophonist David Murray, the drummer Max Roach), and the political content was usually explicit. Jon Jang has put the connection cleanly: he learned from Black mentors like Max Roach, Amiri Baraka, and Horace Tapscott that, as James Baldwin had said, "this music begins on the auction block."

In 1987, Jang and Wong founded a small record label called Asian Improv Records (AIR) to put the movement's work into circulation. AIR has released more than thirty recordings since then by Jang, Wong, Izu, Horiuchi, Masaoka, Ho, Iyer, and others. The label is still active. The Chicago saxophonist and producer Tatsu Aoki founded an AIR Midwest branch and the Chicago Asian American Jazz Festival in the 1990s, extending the movement's reach to a second city. Together, the AAJF, AIR, and a handful of allied venues (Eulipia in San Jose, Yoshi's in Oakland, the Asian Art Museum's auditorium when the festival moved there in the 1980s) built an institutional infrastructure for Asian American jazz that the Movement-era folk-protest of A Grain of Sand had never had.

The political conditions that gave the movement its energy in its first decade were two. The first was the campaign for redress and reparations for Japanese American incarceration. After decades of community organizing led by the Japanese American Citizens League, the National Coalition for Redress and Reparations, and the National Council for Japanese American Redress, Congress established the Commission on Wartime Relocation and Internment of Civilians in 1980, which held hearings in eleven cities with testimony from more than seven hundred and fifty witnesses; published Personal Justice Denied in 1983, which named "race prejudice, war hysteria, and a failure of political leadership" as the causes of the incarceration; and recommended monetary reparations. The , signed by President Reagan on August 10, 1988, granted a formal apology and a $20,000 reparations payment to every surviving Japanese American who had been incarcerated. Jang's 1987 work Reparations Now! Concerto for Jazz Ensemble and Taiko was composed inside this campaign and toured through it.

The second political condition was the murder of Vincent Chin. On the night of June 19, 1982, Chin, a twenty-seven-year-old Chinese American draftsman, was at his bachelor party at the Fancy Pants Club in Highland Park, Michigan. Two white autoworkers, the Chrysler foreman Ronald Ebens and his stepson Michael Nitz, who had recently been laid off from the same plant, were also at the club. Detroit's auto industry was deep in recession; Japanese auto imports were widely (and incorrectly) blamed by US autoworkers for the layoffs. A witness later testified that Ebens shouted at Chin, "It's because of you motherfuckers that we're out of work." Ebens and Nitz pursued Chin out of the club and into the street, found him at a nearby McDonald's, and beat him with a baseball bat. Chin died of his injuries four days later, on June 23, 1982. Ebens and Nitz pleaded guilty to manslaughter; Wayne County Judge Charles Kaufman sentenced them to three years' probation and a three-thousand-dollar fine, with no jail time, explaining that "these weren't the kind of men you send to jail."

The lenient sentence catalyzed Asian American civil rights organizing. In Detroit, the journalist Helen Zia, the lawyer Liza Chan, and Chin's mother Lily Chin formed American Citizens for Justice in March 1983 and successfully petitioned the Department of Justice to bring federal civil rights charges. The 1984 federal trial was the first time the Department of Justice had argued that the civil rights of an Asian American had been violated. The trial convicted Ebens; an appeal moved the retrial to Cincinnati in 1987, where a jury acquitted him. Neither Ebens nor Nitz spent a full day in jail. The case became, and remains, the founding case of pan-Asian American civil rights: it was the moment when Chinese Americans, Japanese Americans, Korean Americans, Filipino Americans, and others came to understand that the racial coding the killers had used (mistaking a Chinese American for Japanese, blaming an entire race for an industrial recession) made the panethnic Asian American identity not just a political choice but a survival condition.

This module's second track, Jon Jang's "Are You Chinese or Charlie Chan?", was composed in direct response to Chin's murder. Jang has been explicit about that: "In 1984 I made the record Are You Chinese or Charlie Chan? as a direct response to the 1982 murder of Vincent Chin in Detroit. It was the beginning of my process of documenting my experiences as an activist in the Asian American Movement." The album was recorded in San Francisco for RPM Records (an early San Francisco indie label that released two of Jang's first three albums before AIR was founded) and mastered at Fantasy Studios in Berkeley. The septet on the recording is the early Asian American Jazz movement assembling itself: Jang on piano, Mark Izu on bass and sheng, Francis Wong on tenor saxophone, Fred Wei-Han Houn (Fred Ho) on baritone saxophone, Anthony Brown on drums, Cash Killion on cello. Almost everyone in the room would go on to be a major figure in the movement. The title's question (are you Chinese or Charlie Chan?) names the choice the murder had made unavoidable. is a fictional Chinese American detective from a 1925 novel series who was played in more than forty Hollywood films, almost always by white actors in ; Chan is the white American imagination of what a Chinese American man is. Vincent Chin had been killed because his murderers saw him through a version of that imagination. The question Jang's title poses is whether Asian Americans can claim themselves on their own terms or whether they remain trapped inside the white imagination of who they are. The listening guide will get into the music. The framing reading should hold onto the political weight the question carries.

The Jets, the Minneapolis sound, and the visibility question

The third track of this module sits in a different musical world from the first two and asks a different question of the Asian American category. Where A Grain of Sand made Asian American identity audible by writing songs about it, and where Jon Jang's septet made it audible by combining Asian instruments with African American jazz forms, The Jets put eight Tongan American siblings in front of a microphone, gave them a Minneapolis-sound R&B production, and sent them up the Billboard Hot 100. The Jets' identity is not in the music. It is in the band itself.

The Wolfgramm family came to the United States from the Kingdom of Tonga in the late 1960s. Maikeli and Vela Wolfgramm immigrated under the post-1965 Hart-Celler admissions and raised seventeen children, eight of whom (LeRoy, Eddie, Eugene, Haini, Rudy, Kathi, Elizabeth, and Moana) became the original Jets. The family started as a Polynesian-style touring act in Salt Lake City and Hawaii, watching The Jacksons and The Osmonds on television and dreaming about a similar career for themselves. They relocated to Minneapolis in the early 1980s after a Midwestern hotel chain that had been booking them went bankrupt and stranded them in the area. They were discovered by the Minneapolis impresario Don Powell, who became their manager, and signed to MCA Records in 1984. Their self-titled debut came out in October 1985.

The album was produced by Powell with David Z (David Rivkin), the engineer who had worked on Prince's Purple Rain the year before, and recorded between Nicollet Studios in Minneapolis and Sunset Sound in Los Angeles. Its sonic palette is the late : synths front and center, programmed drums with the era-specific snare snap, clean funk guitar scratches, layered vocal harmonies, and the kind of slick crossover pop production that put Prince, the Time, and Jam and Lewis productions for the SOS Band and Janet Jackson at the top of the charts in the same years. The teenage Wolfgramms harmonized as siblings only can. "Crush on You," the album's second single, peaked at number three on the Hot 100 and number four on the Hot R&B/Hip-Hop chart in the spring of 1986. Three more Jets singles reached the Hot 100 top ten in the next four years. The Jets were the first group of Pacific Islander descent to crack the pop top ten.

Listen to "Crush on You" without knowing anything about the band, and there is almost nothing in the music that would tell you the singers are Tongan American. The song is a competent piece of mid-1980s teen pop, sung by what could have been any racially ambiguous family vocal group of the period. The identity is in the bodies on the album cover, in the press cycle that talked about the Tongan family, in the parents who came to the United States after 1965, and in the eight siblings who carried that heritage as biographical fact rather than musical content. The Jets are an example of Asian Pacific Islander identity at work in the structural sense (the family's migration story, the post-1965 demographic moment, the multiethnic Minneapolis music scene that Black artists like Prince had built and that opened the door to Pacific Islander R&B) without that identity being audible in the sound itself.

That is itself a teaching question, and it is part of what this module's anchor lineup is trying to make audible. Some of Asian American music sounds like Asian American music: the trio on A Grain of Sand is singing songs about being Asian American, and Jang's septet is fusing Chinese instruments with African American jazz. Other Asian American music does not sound like Asian American music, in any audibly thematized way: it sounds like Minneapolis-sound R&B or like indie rock or like turntablism, performed by people whose Asian American identity is part of the conditions that produced the music but is not part of the music's audible content. Both kinds count. The Asian American category is broad enough to include both, and one of the things this module wants to do is make the difference between them legible. When you get to the Track 3 listening guide, the prompts will ask you to think about what is in the music and what is in the bodies and biographies that produced it, and how to hold both at once without collapsing one into the other.

One more thing about The Jets that is worth flagging here. The Wolfgramms are Tongan, which means they are rather than Asian in the strictest sense. The federal AAPI category that gathers Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders into a single bureaucratic grouping is, as the first section of this reading noted, contested by many Pacific Islander organizers because Pacific Islander histories are different in important ways: they are histories of Indigenous peoples whose lands were colonized by the United States rather than histories of immigration. Including The Jets in this module is a choice the course is making with that complication in mind. The Tongan diaspora to the United States is a different kind of arrival than the Tongan Indigenous experience in the United States' Pacific territories; the Wolfgramms were immigrants in the conventional sense. But the bigger question (whether Pacific Islander pop belongs inside an "Asian American" frame at all) is one that the course wants you to be able to ask, not one it wants to settle for you.

Filipino American mobile DJ crews and the invention of turntablism

The fourth track of this module returns to the Bay Area, but to a different Bay Area than the one Track 2 came out of. Where Jon Jang's Asian American Jazz scene was concentrated in San Francisco's Chinatown and the political networks of the I-Hotel survivors, the scene that produced Track 4 is concentrated in the Filipino American suburbs around the Bay: Daly City, just south of San Francisco; Vallejo, on the northeast edge of the Bay; Union City, Fremont, and Hayward in the East Bay; San Jose at the southern end. These are the places where the post-1965 Filipino diaspora to the Bay Area landed, in numbers large enough that Daly City became known by the late 1980s as the highest concentration of Filipinos anywhere in the world outside of metro Manila. The scene that produced this module's fourth track grew up inside those suburbs, in living rooms and garages and high school gyms, with a pair of turntables and a mixer bought through Filipino American family networks and a generation of DJs who treated those tools as instruments rather than as playback devices.

The historian Oliver Wang's 2015 book Legions of Boom: Filipino American Mobile DJ Crews in the San Francisco Bay Area (Duke University Press) is the definitive account of this scene. It runs from the late 1970s through the mid-1990s and centers on what Wang calls the Filipino American mobile DJ crews, groups of DJs who pooled equipment to play parties, debutante balls, weddings, and high school dances throughout Bay Area Filipino communities. The crews had names: Ultimate Creations, Spintronix, Images Inc., Audio Express, Sound Explosion, Non-Stop Boogie, Boombox Productions, and dozens more. They competed with each other for bookings; they hosted DJ battles in which crews would face off against each other across pairs of turntables; they trained successive generations of teenage DJs through informal apprenticeship. The mobile DJ scene was not the only Filipino American youth culture of the era (car clubs, dance crews, and youth gangs were also part of the same suburban world), but it was the one that produced a musical practice the rest of American popular music has since absorbed.

The musical practice is : the use of two turntables and a mixer to manipulate recorded sound in real time, treating the turntable as a percussion-and-pitch instrument rather than as a playback device. The vocabulary is by now widely known. The DJ moves a record back and forth under the needle to produce a percussive scratch sound; uses the crossfader on the mixer to chop that scratch into rhythmic patterns; cues a second record on the second turntable to extend a four-bar drum break indefinitely (the "" technique that Bronx hip hop pioneers like Kool Herc and Grandmaster Flash had developed in the early-to-mid 1970s); juxtaposes records to make sonic collages; uses scratching to play melodic phrases by altering the pitch of held vowels and tones. Turntablism is not a Filipino American invention in any originating sense. The technique was invented in the by Black and Puerto Rican DJs in the 1970s. The Filipino American Bay Area scene of the 1980s did not invent it; the scene picked up the technique from records and from television, brought it into a different geography, and then pushed it further than the Bronx originators had pushed it, into a virtuoso-instrumental practice that valued speed, precision, complex rhythmic patterning, and ensemble playing across multiple turntables.

By the late 1980s, that virtuoso turn was concentrated in a small group of DJs from Daly City, San Francisco, and the surrounding cities. The most important of them was Richard Quitevis, who recorded as ; his close collaborators included Mike Schwartz (Mix Master Mike) and Jonathan Cruz (Shortkut). In 1989, Q-Bert, Mix Master Mike, and DJ Apollo formed the crew that would soon be called the . Shortkut and DJ Disk joined in subsequent years. The Piklz won the DMC World DJ Championship three years in a row and were retroactively named DMC Champions of the Decade after the DMC organization asked them to retire from competition. They appeared on the Beastie Boys' Hello Nasty in 1998, released solo and crew records on Asphodel and other labels through the 1990s, and produced a body of recorded turntablism that treated the turntable as a serious composer-performer instrument. Q-Bert's Wave Twisters (1998), an album-length turntablist concept record with an animated film attached, is among the most ambitious recorded turntablism documents ever made. The crew's practice and pedagogy (Q-Bert's instructional videos, Mix Master Mike's collaborations with the Beastie Boys, Shortkut's later work with the Beat Junkies) shaped the broader hip hop turntablism scene through the 1990s and into the 2000s. The Skratch Piklz are now widely understood as one of the central crews in the history of recorded turntablism.

The point of telling this story inside an Asian American music module is not just to claim the Skratch Piklz as Asian American (they are; Q-Bert, Mix Master Mike, and Shortkut are all Filipino American). It is also to make the social infrastructure visible. The Daly City living rooms and the Union City garages and the Filipino Cultural Center on the SF State campus and the high school dance circuit and the mobile DJ crew apprenticeships were all part of a Filipino American world built by post-1965 immigrants. Most of those immigrants came through the family-reunification clause of the Hart-Celler Act and the medical and engineering professions the law's employment preferences favored. The DJs who turned the turntable into an instrument in the late 1980s were the children of those immigrants, most of them teenagers. They were doing what generations of teenagers had done with the music available to them, which is to say they were taking African American hip hop, finding it electrifying, and making something out of it that nobody had quite made before. Their location at the suburban edge of San Francisco rather than at the urban center of New York is part of why their version of turntablism developed the way it did: more time at home with two turntables, more practice hours, less of the pressure to be hooked into a commercial rap industry that needed vocalists rather than virtuoso DJs. The Filipino American mobile DJ scene is one of the clearest examples in this module of post-1965 demographic change producing a musical practice the rest of the country eventually had to learn from.

The connection to Module 3's Latin and Caribbean traditions is direct enough to flag. The Filipino American Joe Bataan, who appears in Module 3 as a Latin soul and boogaloo bandleader, is part of the same broader Filipino American musical history this module is now picking up at a different point. Bataan was working in 1960s and 1970s New York at the intersection of Black American and Puerto Rican music, decades before the Bay Area mobile DJ scene developed. The Skratch Piklz were not playing Bataan records (or not specifically those) but they were doing something analogous in a different decade and a different place: taking the dominant Black-and-Latino popular music form available to them and inserting Filipino American musicians into its center. The shape of Filipino American participation in American popular music has often been like this. It is rarely a separate tradition with its own audible markers; more often it is Filipino American musicians moving inside Black American forms with enough virtuosity that the form ends up changing because of them.

The geographic neighborhood matters too. In April 2016, the San Francisco Board of Supervisors formally established , a Filipino Cultural Heritage District covering 1.5 square miles of South of Market. The designation is partly an acknowledgment of more than a century of Filipino presence in San Francisco (Manilatown's pre-WWII Kearny Street community, the post-1965 SOMA settlement, the Bindlestiff Studio performance space, the Mint Mall Filipino business hub) and partly a working response to the displacement that Bay Area gentrification has been imposing on the community for the past three decades. The Skratch Piklz scene was happening in the suburbs around San Francisco rather than inside SOMA itself, but the SOMA Pilipinas designation and the social-cultural infrastructure it makes visible (the Filipino American National Historical Society's Pinoy Archives, the annual Pistahan Festival, the constellation of community-arts spaces in SOMA and the East Bay) are continuous with the world that produced the DJs. The listening guide for Track 4 will go into the music itself. What is worth holding onto from the framing reading is that the Bay Area Filipino American scene is one of the few Asian American musical worlds in this module that has produced both a major virtuoso practice (turntablism) and a recognized urban cultural geography (SOMA Pilipinas, Daly City as the unofficial capital of the Filipino American Bay Area).

Mitski and what 2016 sounds like

The fifth and final track of this module is the most recent and the most singular. , who records under just her first name, is a Japanese American singer and songwriter who released her fourth album Puberty 2 on June 17, 2016, on the Bloomington-based independent label Dead Oceans. The lead single, "Your Best American Girl," came out on March 1, 2016, almost exactly forty-three years after Iijima, Miyamoto, and Chin entered A-1 Sound Studios to record A Grain of Sand. The two records bracket this module: Iijima's announces the new identity, Mitski's lives inside it, after several decades of accumulation and inheritance and complication.

Mitski's biography is mobile in a way the other musicians on this module's lineup are not. Born in Mie Prefecture, Japan, on September 27, 1990, to a Japanese mother and an American father whose work for the US State Department took the family overseas, she grew up between Japan, China, Malaysia, the Czech Republic, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Turkey, and several US locations, attending thirteen schools across multiple countries before college. Her first language was Japanese. She has described herself as "half Japanese, half American but not fully either." She came to the United States as a young adult to study film at Hunter College, then transferred to the SUNY Purchase Conservatory of Music to study studio composition; she graduated in 2013. Her stage name combines her first name (Mitsuki, shortened to Mitski) with her mother's maiden name (Miyawaki). She self-released her first two albums (Lush in 2012 and Retired from Sad, New Career in Business in 2013) as student projects at Purchase, the second of which was a senior project that involved a sixty-piece orchestra. After graduation, she signed to the small Brooklyn label Double Double Whammy for Bury Me at Makeout Creek in 2014, and to Dead Oceans for Puberty 2 in 2016. Puberty 2 was the breakthrough. Pitchfork named "Your Best American Girl" Best New Track; NPR Music named it the second-best song of 2016, after Beyoncé's "Formation."

The song was recorded over two weeks at Acme Studios in Westchester County, New York, with the producer Patrick Hyland, who has produced almost all of Mitski's records since Bury Me at Makeout Creek. Its formal structure is what makes it musically remarkable. The first verse is acoustic guitar and voice, sparse and quiet, tracking the singer's growing sense of distance from a romantic interest. The chorus, when it arrives, is electric: distorted guitars, programmed drums, the kind of wall-of-sound that quotes 1990s alternative rock (the Pixies, Nirvana, the loud-quiet-loud song-form that had dominated American indie rock for two decades) without exactly imitating any specific source. The song moves repeatedly between those two registers (quiet acoustic verses, loud distorted choruses) with the singer's voice tracking a corresponding emotional arc: confessional and uncertain in the verses, defiant and triumphant in the choruses. It is a song that Asian American kids who grew up with 1990s indie rock recognize immediately as belonging to their world.

The lyric is autobiographical. Mitski has said she wrote it while in love with someone whose American whiteness made the relationship impossible: "I didn't grow up in the US. I am half Japanese, and it came from wanting to just fit into this very American person's life and simply not being able to. Just fundamentally being from a different place." The chorus moves between the speaker's mother and the boy's mother and the speaker's growing self-acceptance: "Your mother wouldn't approve of how my mother raised me, but I do, I finally do." The Zia Anger-directed music video, released April 13, 2016, externalizes the song's premise. Mitski sits on a stool in a magenta pantsuit; an "all-American boy" sits on a stool across the frame; a "Coachella-coded" white woman in a flower crown enters and the boy forgets Mitski exists; Mitski waves into empty space, then begins to make out with her own hand, then begins shredding a distorted guitar. The video is funny and angry and self-aware; it knows what kind of music video it is parodying and what kind of indie-rock world Mitski is making her place inside.

Mitski herself has been careful about how the song's politics get framed. When critics responded by reading "Your Best American Girl" as a feminist or anti-racist anthem aimed at the white indie-rock world, she pushed back. "I wasn't trying to send a message," she wrote on Facebook in 2016. "I was in love." But she has also said, in an interview with Minnesota Public Radio's The Current, "Everything is an undercurrent. No woman of color can be in love without it being political. I can't walk through the world without being a political entity just by being who I am. So when I fall in love, it is political." The two statements together describe what is happening in the song. The song is a love song. It is also a song about being a half-Japanese woman in the United States in 2016. The two cannot be separated, because the speaker cannot separate them, because the speaker is the same person living both at once.

That is what makes "Your Best American Girl" a fitting closing track for this module. The song does not announce that it is Asian American music in the way A Grain of Sand did. It does not insert a Chinese instrument into a jazz ensemble in the way Jang's septet did. It does not foreground the body of an immigrant family in the way The Jets did, and it does not redirect a Black-and-Puerto-Rican musical form through a Filipino American virtuoso scene in the way the Skratch Piklz did. It does something quieter and harder to name: it lets the speaker's specificity be present in the music without being thematized. The song's Asian Americanness is in the words, in the quiet tension between the speaker and the all-American boy, in the singer's voice and biography and self-presentation, and in the structural fact that an indie-rock song this self-aware about identity could not have been a top-of-the-decade song in the same way in 1976 or 1986 or 1996. It is a 2016 song. It assumes a listener who has already done the work of recognizing that identity is part of how a song means.

Mitski's career since 2016 has continued to push at this question. Her subsequent albums (Be the Cowboy in 2018, Laurel Hell in 2022, The Land Is Inhospitable and So Are We in 2023, Nothing's About to Happen to Me in 2026) have widened her audience considerably; she is now one of the most recognized Asian American musicians working in indie rock, and "My Love Mine All Mine" from The Land became her first Hot 100 single in 2023 after an unexpected TikTok virality. The Track 5 listening guide will go into "Your Best American Girl" itself. What is worth holding onto from the framing reading is that this song is what an established, post-Movement Asian American identity sounds like in the indie-rock idiom. It is forty-three years downstream from A Grain of Sand; it is also, in its own way, made possible by it.

What this module leaves out

Five anchor tracks cannot cover Asian American music. The lineup this module follows tries to make a particular argument (that the panethnic Asian American category was invented in 1968 and that the music made under that banner has had a layered relationship to the category ever since), and the choices the lineup makes in service of that argument leave out a great deal. A short list, with the understanding that everything on this list is either covered later in the course or worth the kind of independent project the term ahead will give you space for.

South Asian American popular musics. The Indian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi, and Sri Lankan American communities that grew rapidly after 1965 have produced major contributions to American popular music that this module does not cover: bhangra and the bhangra-and-hip-hop fusions that came out of New York's Punjabi diaspora in the 1990s and 2000s, the South Asian American hip hop of Heems and the Das Racist generation, the Tamil American hip hop scene around M.I.A.'s collaborators, the Indian American jazz of Vijay Iyer (whose work touches the Asian American Jazz movement above but extends well past it), the South Asian American R&B of Raveena and Joy Crookes (the latter is British, but the diaspora is networked across the Atlantic), the contemporary South Asian American pop of Saweetie, Raja Kumari, and others. Module 6 picks up some of this work in its contemporary cross-tradition section. The framing reading and the listening guides for the rest of this module assume that students who want a more South-Asian-centered Asian American music story will pursue it for the final project.

Vietnamese American, Hmong American, Cambodian American, Lao American popular musics. The Southeast Asian refugee diasporas that began arriving after the 1975 Indochina Migration and Refugee Assistance Act have produced their own substantial popular musics: the Paris by Night Vietnamese American variety-show tradition that ran continuously from 1983 through the 2010s, the Hmong American rap and pop scene centered in Minneapolis-Saint Paul and Fresno, the Cambodian American psychedelic rock revival around Dengue Fever (a partly-Khmer band) and the broader effort to reconstruct the Cambodian rock scene the Khmer Rouge tried to erase, the Lao American hip hop scene around Thavius Beck and others. None of these traditions appears in this module. The Southeast Asian American refugee experience deserves more than a single anchor track could give it, and a fuller treatment is one of the most defensible projects a student could choose for the final.

and the contemporary Asian American reception of Korean popular music. K-pop is Korean popular music, made in South Korea and exported globally; it is not Asian American music in any straightforward sense. The decision to leave it out of this module's anchor lineup is deliberate: the Asian American musics this module is covering are made in the United States by Asian Americans, and the K-pop case (made in South Korea, consumed enthusiastically in the US, with significant Asian American fan participation) is a different kind of question. The scene, which has marketed Asian and Asian American hip hop and R&B (Rich Brian, Joji, NIKI, Higher Brothers) to American audiences since the late 2010s, is closer to the module's frame and would make a strong final project. So would the contemporary Asian American hip hop scene that runs from the Mountain Brothers in the 1990s through Far East Movement, Dumbfoundead, Awkwafina, MILCK, and others. Module 6 picks up some of this work; the rest is open territory.

The post-2016 contemporary moment. The years after the Mitski track that closes this module have been a period of rapid change in Asian American popular music. The 2020-2021 surge of anti-Asian violence during the COVID-19 pandemic prompted a body of music made in response, including MILCK's "Quiet" and the Stop AAPI Hate compilation work, alongside an increased visibility for Asian American artists across hip hop, R&B, indie rock, and pop. The mainstreaming of Asian American filmmakers (the success of Crazy Rich Asians in 2018, of Lulu Wang's The Farewell in 2019, of Lee Isaac Chung's Minari in 2020, of Daniel Kwan and Daniel Scheinert's Everything Everywhere All at Once in 2022, the latter co-scored by Mitski) has reshaped the cultural visibility of the category in ways popular music has only begun to register. Module 6 returns to some of this contemporary moment in its final synthesis section.

The lineup this module follows is one path through Asian American music. It is not the path. Five tracks in 1973, 1984, 1986, the 1990s, and 2016 sketch a trajectory that the rest of the module's reading and listening will spend the next two weeks filling in. The student final project is the place to follow the trajectory somewhere this module did not go.
