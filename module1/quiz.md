# Module 1 Checkpoint Quiz

A short quiz covering the methodology reading, the cultural roots framing, and the four anchor tracks. Twelve questions, multiple choice, low-stakes. Students may retake the quiz as many times as they want; highest score counts.

The structured version below is for reading. The "PASTE INTO CANVAS" block further down has each question in plain text, ready to paste one at a time when building the Canvas quiz. The answer key is at the very bottom for instructor reference and should not be pasted into Canvas.

## Quiz introduction (paste into the Canvas quiz description box)

Welcome to the Module 1 Checkpoint Quiz. This quiz is short and low-stakes. It is here to make sure the methodology reading, the cultural roots framing, and the four anchor tracks have landed before you move on to the rest of the course.

A few things to know before you start:

There are 12 multiple-choice questions. Plan for about 15 to 20 minutes, though you can take as long as you need. There is no time limit.

You can retake this quiz as many times as you want. Your highest score is the one that counts. If you miss a question, go back to the relevant reading or listening guide, then come back and try again. The point is for you to actually learn this material, not to be punished for not knowing it on the first try.

The questions cover three things: the four listening concepts from the methodology reading (timbre, texture, form, gesture), the cultural roots and traditions framing, and specific facts and contexts from the four anchor tracks (Cooke, Cruz, DeSanto, Williams). Most questions ask you to apply a concept to one of the tracks rather than just recall a definition.

You should complete the readings and the listening guide before taking this quiz. The quiz is the last thing you do in Module 1, after the discussion post.

If something on the quiz seems unclear or you think a question is wrong, email me. I would rather hear about it than have you stuck.

Good luck.

## Questions (structured for reading)

### 1.

In the methodology reading, the term "timbre" was defined as the quality or color of a sound. Which of the following is best described as a timbre choice?

a. Sam Cooke's decision to release "A Change Is Gonna Come" as a single in 1964.
b. The way Sugar Pie DeSanto sometimes shouts a line rather than singing it.
c. The fact that Hank Williams's song has three verses and no chorus.
d. The use of a smoky, gospel-trained vocal sound on a song with orchestral strings.

### 2.

Which of these openings is the clearest example of a thin texture, in the sense the methodology reading uses the term?

a. The opening of Sam Cooke's "A Change Is Gonna Come," with strings underneath and lead vocal above.
b. The opening of Fania All-Stars with Cruz's "Quimbara," with multiple percussion instruments, horns, piano, bass, and chorus all entering close together.
c. The opening of Sugar Pie DeSanto's "I Don't Wanna Fuss," with the full Chess house band already playing.
d. None of the above; all four anchor tracks open with dense textures.

### 3.

The methodology reading distinguishes "form" from "gesture." Which pair below correctly identifies one of each?

a. Form: the verse-chorus-bridge structure of a song. Gesture: the moment a singer's voice cracks on a particular line.
b. Form: the moment a singer's voice cracks. Gesture: the verse-chorus-bridge structure.
c. Both examples are gestures. Form is something else entirely.
d. Both examples are forms. Gesture is something else entirely.

### 4.

The framing reading distinguished migration from displacement. The difference between them is best described as:

a. Migration is voluntary movement; displacement is when people are forced to move.
b. Migration involves crossing national borders; displacement happens within a single country.
c. Migration is a modern phenomenon; displacement is historical.
d. There is no meaningful difference; the words mean the same thing.

### 5.

The framing reading argued that organizing the course around cultural traditions (rather than genres or decades) lets us see something specific. What does this framing make most visible?

a. The communities that made the music and the dialogues between those communities.
b. The chronological order in which different styles emerged.
c. Which artists were most commercially successful in their era.
d. The technical innovations in recording and production that shaped each era.

### 6.

Two important threads run across multiple modules and get focused attention in Module 6 (the synthesis module) rather than in their own dedicated modules. These threads are:

a. Indigenous popular music and queer liberation.
b. Country music and folk music.
c. Hip hop and electronic dance music.
d. Jazz and blues.

### 7.

Sam Cooke wrote "A Change Is Gonna Come" partly in response to:

a. A specific incident in 1963 in which Cooke and his bandmates were turned away from a whites-only motel in Louisiana.
b. The assassination of John F. Kennedy.
c. Hearing Aretha Franklin sing for the first time.
d. A request from Martin Luther King Jr. for a civil rights anthem.

### 8.

The Fania All-Stars, the ensemble that recorded "Quimbara" with Celia Cruz live in Zaire in 1974, were:

a. A Pan-Latin ensemble made up of Cuban, Puerto Rican, Dominican, Panamanian, and other Latin American musicians signed to Fania Records.
b. A Cuban band that left Cuba together after the 1959 revolution.
c. The house band at a single nightclub in New York City.
d. A studio orchestra assembled only for that one Zaire 74 performance.

### 9.

Sugar Pie DeSanto sits at an intersection of several identities and musical traditions. Which combination most accurately describes her?

a. Filipino and African American, raised in San Francisco, recorded for Chess Records in Chicago.
b. Cuban and African American, raised in Miami, recorded for Atlantic Records.
c. Mexican American, raised in Texas, recorded for Sun Records.
d. African American, raised in Chicago, recorded for Motown.

### 10.

In the Hank Williams listening guide, you encountered a Black street musician in Alabama named Rufus Payne, called "Tee Tot," who:

a. Taught Williams blues phrasing and guitar techniques during Williams's youth.
b. Wrote "I'm So Lonesome I Could Cry" and sold the song to Williams.
c. Played in Williams's touring band throughout the 1940s.
d. Recorded the original version of the song before Williams.

### 11.

Of the four Module 1 anchor tracks, which one most directly represents the African American foundational traditions thread of the course?

a. Sam Cooke, "A Change Is Gonna Come."
b. Hank Williams, "I'm So Lonesome I Could Cry."
c. Fania All-Stars with Cruz, "Quimbara."
d. All four tracks represent that thread equally.

### 12.

The four Module 1 anchor tracks together preview the course's four cultural traditions. Which tradition is represented by Sugar Pie DeSanto's "I Don't Wanna Fuss"?

a. Asian American traditions, in dialogue with African American R&B.
b. European American immigrant and working-class traditions.
c. Latin diasporic traditions.
d. None of the four traditions; her work sits outside the course's framing.

# Answer key (instructor reference; do not paste into Canvas)

- **Q1: d.** A vocal sound and an orchestral arrangement are both timbre choices; they describe the quality of the sound itself. (a) is a release decision, (b) is a gesture, (c) is a formal choice.
- **Q2: a.** The Cooke opening is the canonical thin-texture example used in the methodology reading's Figure 1. The Cruz opening is the dense-texture example. The DeSanto opening uses the full Chess house band, which is a denser texture though not as dense as the Fania ensemble.
- **Q3: a.** Form is structural (how a song is organized in time, including section types like verse and chorus). Gesture is a specific musical moment that carries weight (the voice cracking, the shout, the held note).
- **Q4: a.** The framing reading defined displacement as migration that is not chosen. Migration is movement; displacement is forced movement. The other distractors are not the distinction the reading drew.
- **Q5: a.** The framing reading argued that traditions (which are upstream of genres) make the communities and the dialogues between them visible. Genres are downstream. The other options describe other valid lenses but not what the cultural-traditions framing specifically makes visible.
- **Q6: a.** The framing reading explicitly named these two as the threads picked up in Module 6: Indigenous popular music (Jim Pepper, Robbie Robertson, the Halluci Nation as examples) and queer liberation (the disco era, the AIDS crisis, contemporary artists like Frank Ocean).
- **Q7: a.** The Cooke listening guide established this. The Louisiana motel incident is a documented historical fact and a key piece of context for the song.
- **Q8: a.** The Cruz listening guide established the Pan-Latin nature of the Fania All-Stars. (b) is wrong because Fania was a Pan-Latin label, not a Cuban exile band. (c) and (d) are wrong because Fania was a recording label and the All-Stars toured and recorded extensively.
- **Q9: a.** From the DeSanto listening guide: Filipino and African American, raised in San Francisco, signed to Chess Records in Chicago in the early 1960s.
- **Q10: a.** From the Williams listening guide: Tee Tot was Rufus Payne, a Black street musician who taught Williams blues phrasing and guitar techniques during Williams's youth in Alabama. Williams himself acknowledged the influence; later country music history often did not.
- **Q11: a.** Cooke's track is the African American foundational traditions example among the four. The other tracks represent the other three traditions: Cruz for Latin diasporic, DeSanto for Asian American (in dialogue with Black R&B), Williams for European American working-class.
- **Q12: a.** DeSanto, as Filipino and African American, sits at the intersection of Asian American traditions and African American R&B. The framing reading and the listening guide both name her work as part of the Asian American thread of the course (alongside Joe Bataan, the Invisibl Skratch Piklz, and others), with the African American R&B context that her career also belongs to.
