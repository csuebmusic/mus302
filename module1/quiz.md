# Module 1 Checkpoint Quiz

A short quiz covering the methodology reading, the cultural roots framing, and the four anchor tracks. Twelve questions, multiple choice, low-stakes. You may retake the quiz as many times as you want; your highest score counts.

This file is the source for the quiz; paste the questions into Canvas. The answer key and short rationales are at the bottom of this file for instructor reference. Do not paste the answer key into Canvas.

## 1.

In the methodology reading, the term "timbre" was defined as the quality or color of a sound. Which of the following is best described as a timbre choice?

a. Sam Cooke's decision to release "A Change Is Gonna Come" as a single in 1964.
b. The way Sugar Pie DeSanto sometimes shouts a line rather than singing it.
c. The fact that Hank Williams's song has three verses and no chorus.
d. The use of a smoky, gospel-trained vocal sound on a song with orchestral strings.

## 2.

a. The opening of Sam Cooke's "A Change Is Gonna Come," with strings underneath and lead vocal above.
b. The opening of Fania All-Stars with Cruz's "Quimbara," with multiple percussion instruments, horns, piano, bass, and chorus all entering close together.
c. The opening of Sugar Pie DeSanto's "I Don't Wanna Fuss," with the full Chess house band already playing.
d. None of the above; all four anchor tracks open with dense textures.

## 3.

The methodology reading distinguishes "form" from "gesture." Which pair below correctly identifies one of each?

b. Form: the moment a singer's voice cracks. Gesture: the verse-chorus-bridge structure.
c. Both examples are gestures. Form is something else entirely.
d. Both examples are forms. Gesture is something else entirely.

## 4.

## Answer key (instructor reference; do not paste into Canvas)

**1. (d).** A vocal sound and an orchestral arrangement are both timbre choices: they describe the quality of the sound itself. (a) is a release decision, (b) is a gesture, (c) is a formal choice.

**2. (a).** The opening of Cooke is the canonical thin-texture example used in the methodology reading's Figure 1. The Cruz opening is the dense-texture example. The DeSanto opening uses the full Chess house band, which is a denser texture though not as dense as the Fania ensemble.

**3. (a).** Form is structural (how a song is organized in time, including section types like verse and chorus). Gesture is a specific musical moment that carries weight (the voice cracking, the shout, the held note).

**4. (a).** The framing reading defined displacement as "when migration is not chosen. People are forced to move." Migration is movement; displacement is forced movement. The other distractors are not the distinction the reading drew.

**5. (a).** The framing reading argued that traditions (which are upstream of genres) make the communities and the dialogues between them visible. Genres are downstream. The other options describe other valid lenses but not what the cultural-traditions framing specifically makes visible.

**6. (a).** The framing reading explicitly named these two as the threads picked up in Module 6: Indigenous popular music (mentioning Jim Pepper, Robbie Robertson, the Halluci Nation as examples) and queer liberation (mentioning the disco era, the AIDS crisis, contemporary artists like Frank Ocean).

**7. (a).** The Cooke listening guide established this. The Louisiana motel incident is a documented historical fact and a key piece of context for the song. (b), (c), and (d) are all plausible-sounding but not what happened.

**8. (a).** The Cruz listening guide established the Pan-Latin nature of the Fania All-Stars. (b) is wrong because Fania was a Pan-Latin label, not a Cuban exile band. (c) and (d) are wrong because Fania was a recording label and the All-Stars toured and recorded extensively.

**9. (a).** From the DeSanto listening guide: Filipino and African American, raised in San Francisco, signed to Chess Records in Chicago in the early 1960s.

**10. (a).** From the Williams listening guide: Tee Tot was Rufus Payne, a Black street musician who taught Williams blues phrasing and guitar techniques during Williams's youth in Alabama. Williams himself acknowledged the influence; later country music history often did not.

**11. (a).** Cooke's track is the African American foundational traditions example among the four. The other tracks represent the other three traditions: Cruz for Latin diasporic, DeSanto for Asian American (in dialogue with Black R&B), Williams for European American working-class.

**12. (a).** DeSanto, as Filipino and African American, sits at the intersection of Asian American traditions and African American R&B. The framing reading and the listening guide both name her work as part of the Asian American thread of the course (alongside Joe Bataan, the Invisibl Skratch Piklz, and others), with the African American R&B context that her career also belongs to.
