# Project Proposal and Preliminary Bibliography (Checkpoint 1 of 4)

The first of four graded checkpoints for the final project. Due Sunday, June 28 at 11:59 PM Pacific. The goal of this checkpoint is to commit to a topic, locate enough sources to confirm the topic is researchable, and let me flag any concerns early enough to course-correct.

This checkpoint replaces what used to be two separate checkpoints (a proposal and a fuller annotated bibliography). The annotation work that used to live in the bibliography moves into the draft itself, where students discuss sources in context as part of the deck.

Full project information is at https://csuebmusic.github.io/mus302/final-project/.

## What students submit

A short proposal of roughly 300 to 500 words, plus a preliminary bibliography of three to four sources with a one-sentence description of each.

## Rubric (out of 35 points)

### Proposal (20 points)

Five points for clearly naming a genre and a specific artist or group within that genre.

Five points for explaining personal significance: why this topic, what draws the student to it, what they hope to find in researching it. The disclosure does not have to be deeply personal; the explanation does have to be substantive.

Five points for a paragraph framing the argument the project will make. This is new since the old proposal-only checkpoint, and it is the most important addition. Even a tentative argument ("I want to argue that this artist's work made X possible," "I want to show how this song does Y") gives the rest of the project a spine.

Five points for written quality: clear prose, paragraphs not bullet lists, free of obvious errors.

### Preliminary bibliography (15 points)

Eight points for source quality. Three to four sources, with a mix of source types when possible (a book or scholarly article, journalism, a primary source like an interview or liner notes, a well-cited reference article). AI-generated content is not a source.

Five points for source statements. Each source gets one sentence describing what kind of source it is and how the student expects to use it. Specifics, not generalities; "Treats the artist's early gospel period in detail; useful for the section on her musical training" is on the right track.

Two points for citation consistency. Use any citation style consistently across the bibliography.

## Outcomes addressed

Course SLO 6 (research and present a genre and artist of personal significance). GE-UD-3 outcome 1 (apply the methodologies of the arts and humanities). Diversity overlay outcome 3 (examine the impact of one's own identity on experiences with or views of other cultural groups).

## Feedback turnaround

I will return feedback within one week of the deadline. Students should read the feedback before starting work on the draft (due July 19). The most common course corrections at this stage are: a topic that needs to narrow, a topic that needs to broaden, a source list that leans too heavily on one kind of source, and a personal-significance angle that has not yet found its argument.

## Notes for me

This checkpoint is doing two jobs at once. The proposal half is straightforward: confirm a topic, hear the argument the student wants to make, flag concerns. The bibliography half is lighter than the old standalone annotated bibliography (3-4 sources rather than 5-7, one sentence each rather than a fuller annotation), because the annotation work has moved into the draft itself, where students discuss sources in context in the deck.

The students who get into trouble at the draft stage are usually the ones who treated this checkpoint as a formality. Build in time to reach out to students whose preliminary bibliographies suggest a topic shift might help. Better to have that conversation here than at the draft.

# PASTE INTO CANVAS BELOW THIS LINE

The first of four graded checkpoints for your final project. Full project information is at https://csuebmusic.github.io/mus302/final-project/. Read the project page if you have not already; this assignment assumes it.

What to submit

Two pieces in a single submission: a short written proposal and a preliminary bibliography.

The proposal, roughly 300 to 500 words, does four things:

Names your genre and a specific artist or group within that genre. Be specific. "Latin music" is too broad; "1970s salsa" is workable; "the music of Hector Lavoe in the 1970s salsa boom" is excellent. Same with the artist: a single artist or a single group, not a list.

Explains why this topic matters to you. The project asks for personal significance, which can mean many things: music you grew up with, music you discovered that changed you, music your community made, music outside your tradition that you have come to care about. The project page describes these angles in more detail. The explanation does not have to be deeply personal, but it does have to be substantive: why this artist, why now, what draws you to the work.

Frames the argument you want the project to make. This is new since the version of this checkpoint that some of you may have heard about from other terms. Even a tentative argument is fine ("I want to argue that this artist's work made X possible," or "I want to show how this song does Y, against the more common reading that it does Z"). The argument can change between now and the final, but having an argument in mind from the start gives the rest of the project a spine, and it is the thing that makes the difference between a good project and a project that is just a biography summary.

Stays focused. 300 to 500 words is the target. Significantly shorter than 300 usually does not give me enough to give useful feedback on; significantly longer than 500 usually has not yet narrowed down the topic enough.

The preliminary bibliography lists three to four sources, with one sentence on each.

A full citation. Use any consistent citation style (MLA, Chicago, APA, the citation style your major uses, the style I use in the listening guide Sources sections, whatever you like). Be consistent across the whole bibliography.

A one-sentence description of each source. The sentence should name what kind of source it is (book, journal article, journalism, documentary, liner notes, archive material, interview) and say briefly how you expect to use it. "Treats the artist's early gospel period in detail; I will use this for the section on her musical training" is a good model. "Background on the genre" is too vague.

What counts as a source

Books, scholarly articles, peer-reviewed journals, in-depth journalism (NPR, The New Yorker, Rolling Stone profiles, equivalents in other languages), documentaries, podcasts that engage seriously with their material, liner notes, museum websites, archival materials. Album reviews from a real publication count. Interviews count. Academic dissertations count. Well-cited reference articles (Wikipedia, Britannica, and similar) count, with the caveat below.

What does not count: AI-generated text, undocumented blog posts, fan wiki entries, summaries that do not link back to a verifiable source. These can be useful for orienting yourself early in research; they cannot be cited as sources.

A note on Wikipedia. Wikipedia is acceptable as a cited source when the article you are using has substantive footnotes that lead to primary or scholarly material. When you cite a Wikipedia article, you are also expected to follow at least one of its footnotes down to a primary source and include that primary source in your bibliography too. This is good research practice generally, and it is how the listening guides on this site were built.

Why only three or four sources at this stage

The bibliography is preliminary on purpose. The point is to confirm that real sources exist for your topic, not to lock in everything you will use. The fuller research happens between this checkpoint and the draft. By the draft (July 19), your working bibliography will be larger, and you will discuss sources in context inside the deck rather than as a separate exercise.

How this is graded

Thirty-five points total.

The proposal is twenty points: five for clearly naming the genre and artist, five for the explanation of personal significance, five for the paragraph framing your argument, and five for written quality.

The bibliography is fifteen points: eight for source quality (relevance, mix of source types, credibility), five for the one-sentence source statements (specific about what each source is and how you will use it), and two for citation consistency.

What happens after you submit

I read every submission and give you written feedback within one week. The feedback might confirm that your topic is on track, or it might suggest a narrower focus, a richer angle, sources to look at, or sources to drop. Read the feedback before starting on the draft (due July 19).

If you are stuck choosing a topic, email me before the deadline rather than submitting late. This checkpoint is here to help, not to gatekeep.

AI use

Allowed with disclosure, per the course policy. If you used AI to brainstorm, draft, organize sources, or edit, end your submission with a one-sentence acknowledgment of what tool you used and for what purpose. AI-generated text does not count as a source.
