The first of five graded checkpoints for the final project. Due Sunday, June 14 at 11:59 PM Pacific. Worth a small portion of the project grade; the goal of this checkpoint is to commit to a topic and let me flag any concerns early enough to course-correct.

## What students submit

A short proposal, roughly 300 to 500 words, naming their genre and artist, explaining personal significance, and listing two or three preliminary sources.

## Rubric (out of 20 points)

Five points for clearly naming a genre and a specific artist or group within that genre.

Five points for explaining personal significance: why this topic, what draws the student to it, what they hope to find in researching it. The disclosure does not have to be deeply personal; the explanation does have to be substantive.

Five points for two to three preliminary sources, with a sentence or two on each describing what kind of source it is. Books, scholarly articles, journalism, documentaries, liner notes, museum or archive websites, and well-cited reference articles (Wikipedia, Britannica, and similar) all count. AI-generated text does not count as a source.

Five points for written quality: clear prose, paragraphs not bullet lists, free of obvious errors.

Course SLO 6 (research and present a genre and artist of personal significance). GE-UD-3 outcome 1 (apply the methodologies of the arts and humanities). Diversity overlay outcome 3 (examine the impact of one's own identity on experiences with or views of other cultural groups).

I will return feedback within one week of the deadline. Students should read the feedback before starting work on the annotated bibliography (due June 28).

# PASTE INTO CANVAS BELOW THIS LINE

The first of five graded checkpoints for your final project. Full project information is at https://csuebmusic.github.io/mus302/final-project/. Read the project page if you have not already; this assignment assumes it.

What to submit

A short written proposal, roughly 300 to 500 words, that does three things:

Names your genre and a specific artist or group within that genre. Be specific. "Latin music" is too broad; "1970s salsa" is workable; "the music of Hector Lavoe in the 1970s salsa boom" is excellent. Same with the artist: a single artist or a single group, not a list.

Explains why this topic matters to you. The project asks for personal significance, which can mean many things: music you grew up with, music you discovered that changed you, music your community made, music outside your tradition that you have come to care about. The project page describes these angles in more detail. The explanation does not have to be deeply personal, but it does have to be substantive: why this artist, why now, what draws you to the work.

Lists two or three preliminary sources you have already found, with a sentence or two on each describing what kind of source it is and how you expect to use it. Books, scholarly articles, journalism, documentaries, liner notes, podcasts, museum or archive websites, and well-cited reference articles (Wikipedia, Britannica, and similar) all count. AI-generated content does not count as a source.

Length: 300 to 500 words is the target. Proposals significantly shorter than 300 words usually do not give me enough to give useful feedback on. Proposals significantly longer than 500 words usually have not yet narrowed down their topic enough.

How this is graded

Twenty points total. Five for clearly naming the genre and artist. Five for the explanation of personal significance. Five for the preliminary sources. Five for written quality (clear prose, paragraphs not bullets, free of obvious errors).

What happens after you submit

I read every proposal and give you written feedback within one week. The feedback might confirm that your topic is on track, or it might suggest a narrower focus, a richer angle, or sources to look at. Read the feedback before starting on the annotated bibliography (due June 28).

If you are stuck choosing a topic, email me before the deadline rather than submitting late. The proposal is short and low-stakes; it is here to help, not to gatekeep.

AI use

Allowed with disclosure, per the course policy. If you used AI to brainstorm or edit, end your proposal with a one-sentence acknowledgment of what tool you used and for what purpose.
