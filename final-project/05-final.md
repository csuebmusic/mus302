## Rubric (out of 100 points)

This is the rubric for the final, which is also the rubric for the project as a whole. Each section names the outcomes it addresses.

### Argument and analysis (35 points)

The deck and video make a defensible, specific claim about why this artist matters. The claim is supported by sustained musical analysis (specific moments in specific recordings, treated with the kind of detail the Module 1 listening guides modeled) and by cultural and historical context. The argument is not a Wikipedia summary; it is the student's claim, defended.

Outcomes: Course SLOs 1, 2, 3. GE-UD-3 outcomes 1 and 2.

### Genre and cultural context (20 points)

The project introduces the genre with specificity: communities, places, historical conditions, the social and political context the music came out of. Vague gestures at "the times" do not count. The cultural context connects to the artist's specific work, not just to a general background paragraph at the start.

Outcomes: Course SLO 5. GE-UD-3 outcome 2. Diversity overlay outcome 1.

### Source integration (15 points)

Five or more sources from the annotated bibliography are visible in the deck and video, named in the prose where appropriate. Sources include a mix of types (scholarship, journalism, primary documents). Sources are cited in a consistent style on the sources slide. The level of source integration matches what the Module 1 listening guides modeled.

Outcomes: Course SLO 6. GE-UD-3 outcome 1.

### Personal significance (10 points)

The project shows the student's own engagement with the artist and the genre. Personal significance does not have to be a confessional disclosure; it does need to be present in the choices the student made (which recordings to analyze, which angles to emphasize, what they argue matters about this artist). A project that could have been written by anyone for any class earns fewer points than one that visibly belongs to its author.

Outcomes: Course SLO 6. Diversity overlay outcome 3.

### Slide deck quality (10 points)

The deck is 18 to 20 slides total, including title and sources slide, with up to three media slides. Slides are designed (not just walls of text). Images are credited. The deck works as a standalone document.

### Video commentary (10 points)

The video walks through every slide at a reasonable pace. Audio is clear and audible. The student's voice carries the argument, not just narrates the slide content.

## Notes for me

Final grading turnaround should be within two weeks of submission so students have their grades before grade entry deadlines. With 60 students across both sections, plan accordingly: rough timing is 30 to 45 minutes per project for serious grading (watch the video, read through the deck, evaluate against the rubric, write a brief comment). That is 30 to 45 hours total. Splitting the load across the two weeks is necessary.

Use the rubric explicitly when commenting. A short comment (two or three sentences) per project is enough; the rubric does most of the structural work.

How this is graded

The rubric is out of 100 points across six categories. The full rubric is on the final-project page on the course site. The short version:

Argument and analysis: 35 points. Defensible specific claim, sustained analysis of specific recordings, evidence-based argument.

Genre and cultural context: 20 points. Specific communities, places, historical conditions, connected to the artist's work.

Source integration: 15 points. Five or more sources visible in the project, varied in type, cited consistently.

Personal significance: 10 points. The project shows your engagement with the work.

Slide deck quality: 10 points. 18 to 20 slides, designed, images credited, works as a standalone document.

Video commentary: 10 points. Walks through every slide at a reasonable pace, clear audio, your voice carrying the argument.

What the failure modes look like

A few patterns I have seen in past versions of similar projects. Avoid these:

Wikipedia-summary writing. The deck reads like a Wikipedia article on the artist, with no argument and no specific analysis. The fix is to write toward a claim and to point at specific moments in specific recordings.

Background-paragraph context. The cultural and historical context is a single slide at the start, never connected to the artist's work. The fix is to weave context throughout, showing how specific historical conditions shaped specific musical choices.

Source-list-only sources. Sources appear on the sources slide and nowhere else. The fix is to name your sources in the prose. ("Biographer X has argued..." "According to the documentary Y...")

Reading the slides aloud. The video commentary is the student reading the text on each slide. The fix is to think of the slides as the visual support and your voice as the argument; you should be saying things the slides do not say in full.

Going long. The video is 25 minutes when 12 would have done it. There is no maximum length, but: 60 students, one instructor. Pace yourself for clarity, not coverage.

When you turn it in

Test that all three files open and play before you submit. Watch the first thirty seconds of your own video; make sure the audio is audible and the slides are visible.

If something goes wrong technically and you cannot submit on time, email me before the deadline. Late work is accepted with the late penalty in the syllabus, but technical problems are usually solvable if I hear about them early.

AI use

Allowed with disclosure, per the course policy. If you used AI for any part of the final (brainstorming, editing, organizing, troubleshooting), include a brief acknowledgment on the sources slide. The argument, the analysis, and the choice of artist must be yours.
