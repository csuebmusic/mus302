# Peer Review (Checkpoint 4 of 5)

The fourth graded checkpoint. Due Sunday, July 26 at 11:59 PM Pacific. Each student gives structured feedback on two classmates' drafts. The peer review process serves two outcomes at once: the reviewers practice critical reading of project-style work, and the authors get useful feedback to use in the final revision.

Full project information is at https://csuebmusic.github.io/mus302/final-project/.

## How to set this up in Canvas

Canvas's built-in peer review tool can do automated random assignment of two reviewers per submission. Set the peer review to release immediately after the draft deadline closes (Sun, July 19) so students have the full week before the July 26 peer-review deadline to read and respond. Each student should be assigned to review two drafts and should submit each review by the July 26 deadline.

If Canvas's peer review tool is not flexible enough to host the structured form below, set this up as a regular assignment with the form questions in the description and have students submit their two reviews as a single document or as two separate posts. Either works.

## What students submit

Two completed peer reviews, each addressing the structured questions below for the assigned draft.

## Rubric (out of 20 points; 10 per review)

For each review:
- 4 points for completing all the structured questions with substantive answers (not "looks good!").
- 4 points for the constructive comments box: at least one specific suggestion the author could act on in revision.
- 2 points for tone: respectful, specific, oriented toward helping the author rather than judging the draft.

## Outcomes addressed

Course SLOs 1, 2, and 3 (the reviewer practices articulating critical thoughts about creative work and connecting it to context). GE-UD-3 outcome 1.

## The structured form

Each review answers the same six questions about the draft they are reading. The questions are designed to be specific enough to produce useful feedback and short enough that two reviews per student stays sustainable.

# PASTE INTO CANVAS BELOW THIS LINE

The fourth checkpoint for your final project. Due Sunday, July 26 at 11:59 PM Pacific. Full project information is at https://csuebmusic.github.io/mus302/final-project/.

What to submit

Two completed peer reviews. Canvas will assign you two of your classmates' drafts to review. For each one, you will watch the video, look at the deck, and answer the six questions below. You will also receive two reviews of your own draft to use in your final revision.

The point of peer review

You are not grading your classmate's draft. You are giving them feedback they can use to make the final stronger. The questions below are designed to produce useful feedback. Be specific. "I liked it" does not help anyone revise. "The argument in slide 14 surprised me, but I lost track of how it connected to the analysis on slide 9" is useful feedback that the author can act on.

You will get more out of giving good peer reviews than you expect. Reading two complete drafts of the project will sharpen your sense of what makes the project work, which you will then take into your own revision.

The structured form

For each of the two drafts you review, write a response that addresses all six questions. Aim for two to four sentences per question. Keep your tone respectful and specific.

1. What is the author's argument? In your own words, what claim are they making about why this artist matters?

2. Where in the project did the argument feel strongest? Name a specific slide, a specific moment in the video, or a specific piece of evidence the author brought in. Why did it work?

3. Where did the argument feel less developed? Again, be specific. Where did you lose the thread, or want more, or feel that a claim was made without enough evidence?

4. How was the musical analysis? Did the author point at specific moments in specific recordings? Did the analysis use the frames from the methodology reading (timbre, texture, form, gesture)? Where did the analysis work best, and where could it go deeper?

5. How was the cultural and historical context? Did the project connect the artist to specific communities, places, and historical conditions? Was there context you wanted more of?

6. One concrete suggestion for the final revision. Pick the single most useful thing the author could do between now and July 31 to make the final stronger. This is the most important question on the form. Your suggestion should be specific enough that the author can act on it.

How this is graded

Twenty points total, ten per review. For each review:

Four points for completing all six questions with substantive answers. Two-sentence responses are usually substantive; one-line responses usually are not.

Four points for the concrete suggestion in question six. The suggestion should be specific and actionable.

Two points for tone. Respectful, focused on helping the author, not judging.

A note on receiving peer review

You will get two peer reviews of your own draft. Read both of them carefully, even if your first reaction is defensive. Reviewers see things authors cannot see, because authors are too close to the work. Sometimes the reviewer is wrong, and that is fine; their feedback is suggestion, not requirement. But if both reviewers flag the same issue, take that seriously.

Use the peer feedback alongside any draft feedback I have been able to give you. Draft feedback for sixty students takes time; some of you will see my comments before peer review closes, others will see them in the final stretch before the final submission. Either way, between my feedback and your peer feedback, you will have a clear picture of what to focus on in the final revision.

AI use

Peer reviews should be your own writing. Do not use AI to draft your peer feedback for you; the practice of reading critically is part of what this checkpoint teaches. AI is fine for editing your own writing for clarity if you want to use it that way, with disclosure at the end.
