## What students submit

An annotated bibliography of five to seven sources. Each source: full citation in a consistent style, plus one to three sentences describing what the source is and how the student expects to use it in the project.

## Rubric (out of 25 points)

Ten points for source quality. The bibliography should include a mix of source types (books or scholarly articles, journalism, primary documents like interviews or liner notes, archival or museum sources), and each source should be relevant to the project. Wikipedia is not a source. AI-generated content is not a source.

Ten points for annotation quality. Each annotation should describe what kind of source it is (book, journal article, news piece, etc.) and how the student expects to use it. "Background on the genre" is not enough; the annotation should be specific about what the student hopes to learn from this source or what argument it might support.

Five points for written quality and citation consistency.

# PASTE INTO CANVAS BELOW THIS LINE

The second checkpoint for your final project. Builds on your proposal feedback. Full project information is at https://csuebmusic.github.io/mus302/final-project/.

What to submit

An annotated bibliography of five to seven sources. For each source, give:

A full citation. Use any consistent citation style (MLA, Chicago, APA, the citation style your major uses, the style I used in the listening guide Sources sections, whatever you like). Just be consistent across the whole bibliography.

An annotation of one to three sentences. The annotation should do two things: name what kind of source this is (book, journal article, journalism, documentary, liner notes, archive material, etc.) and describe how you expect to use it in the project. "Provides background on the artist" is too vague. Better: "Treats the artist's early gospel period in detail; I will use this for the section on her musical training and the transition to secular pop."

What counts as a source

Books, scholarly articles, peer-reviewed journals, in-depth journalism (NPR, The New Yorker, Rolling Stone profiles, equivalents in other languages), documentaries, podcasts that engage seriously with their material, liner notes, museum websites, archival materials. Album reviews from a real publication count. Interviews count. Academic dissertations count.

What does not count: Wikipedia, AI-generated text, undocumented blog posts, fan wiki entries, summaries that do not link back to a verifiable source. These can be useful for orienting yourself early in research; they cannot be cited as sources.

Variety helps

A bibliography of five sources from the same place tends to flatten the project. Aim for at least three different kinds of source: a book or scholarly article alongside journalism alongside something primary (an interview, liner notes, an archived performance). The Module 1 listening guide Sources sections are a good model for what a varied bibliography looks like.

How this is graded

Twenty-five points total. Ten for source quality (relevance, variety, credibility). Ten for annotation quality (specific about what each source is and how you will use it). Five for written quality and citation consistency.

What happens after you submit

I will return feedback within one week. The feedback may confirm your sources are strong, suggest gaps to fill, or flag sources that turn out to be unreliable. Read the feedback before starting on the draft (due July 12).

A few common mistakes to avoid

Listing five sources but only one is substantive. The other four are quick reference pieces. Try to balance.

Annotations that summarize the source instead of naming what it is and how you will use it. The summary is not the assignment; the use is.

A bibliography that has nothing on the genre, only on the single artist. Both layers of the project (genre context and artist focus) need source support.

AI use

Allowed with disclosure. If you used AI for any part of this work (organizing your sources, drafting annotations, formatting citations), include a one-sentence acknowledgment at the end of your bibliography.
