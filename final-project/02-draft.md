# Full Draft (Checkpoint 2 of 4)

The second graded checkpoint. Due Sunday, July 19 at 11:59 PM Pacific. The largest of the checkpoints in volume of student work, and the one that lets the peer-review and revision cycle do its job.

Full project information is at https://csuebmusic.github.io/mus302/final-project/.

## What students submit

A complete-but-rough version of both deliverables: the full slide deck (18 to 20 slides) and the recorded video walking through it. The draft must be substantively complete (all sections present, an argument visible, evidence in place, sources cited). It does not need to be polished. Polish is what the next two weeks are for.

## Rubric (out of 50 points)

Fifteen points for completeness. All sections present, slide count in the 18 to 20 range, video walks through every slide, sources slide present.

Fifteen points for argument. The deck and video make a defensible claim about why this artist matters. The claim is supported by specific musical evidence and by the cultural and historical context.

Ten points for analysis quality. Specific moments in specific recordings are pointed at and described. The frames from the methodology reading (timbre, texture, form, gesture) are used where they help. Wikipedia-summary writing is the failure mode; specific, source-grounded analysis is the target.

Five points for source integration. Sources from your preliminary bibliography appear in the deck and video, alongside the additional sources you have located in your fuller research between the proposal-and-bibliography checkpoint and the draft. Five or more sources should be visible in the project itself, named in the prose where appropriate, not just listed at the end.

Five points for technical execution at draft stage. The deck opens. The video plays. Audio is audible. Embedded media works. Captions are not required at draft stage.

## Outcomes addressed

All six course SLOs. GE-UD-3 outcomes 1 and 2. Diversity overlay outcome 1 and (where the project engages cross-cultural listening) outcome 3.

## Notes for me

The draft feedback I give here is the longest piece of feedback any student gets in the term. Plan accordingly. The students will use this feedback for their final revision after peer review, so make it specific enough that they know what to revise.

If a student submits a draft that is significantly under the slide minimum or significantly underdeveloped, that is the moment to write directly rather than just give a low score. A draft is fixable; a final submission that fails is not.

# PASTE INTO CANVAS BELOW THIS LINE

The second checkpoint for your final project, and the largest piece of work before the final submission. Due Sunday, July 19 at 11:59 PM Pacific. Full project information is at https://csuebmusic.github.io/mus302/final-project/.

What to submit

A complete first version of both project deliverables: the slide deck and the video commentary. Submit both files together.

The deck

Your deck should be 18 to 20 slides total, including the title slide at the start and the sources slide at the end. Up to three of those slides can carry embedded audio or video media of your artist; the rest should be substantive content slides.

Substantively complete means: all the sections of the project are present (genre introduction, artist introduction, musical analysis, your argument, legacy and connections, sources). The argument is visible, the analysis points at specific moments in specific recordings, and the sources are cited.

Substantively complete does not mean polished. Slides can have placeholder formatting, rough drafts of paragraphs that you know need revision, images that need replacing. The point of the draft is to have a real version of the project that you and your peer reviewers and I can give feedback on. A draft that is too polished to be revised is its own kind of failure.

The video

A complete video walk-through of the deck. There is no minimum or maximum length; the video is as long as it takes to walk through your deck at a reasonable pace. The video does not need to be polished at this stage. Stumbles, pauses, "let me back up and try that again" moments are fine. What I am evaluating at this stage is whether your argument and analysis come through when you talk about your slides, not whether the recording is broadcast-ready.

How this is graded

Fifty points total.

Fifteen for completeness. All sections present, slide count in range, video walks through every slide, sources slide present.

Fifteen for argument. You have a defensible claim about why this artist matters. The claim is supported by specific musical evidence and by cultural and historical context.

Ten for analysis quality. You point at specific moments in specific recordings and describe what is happening. You use the methodology reading's frames (timbre, texture, form, gesture) where they help. The failure mode here is Wikipedia-summary writing; the target is specific, source-grounded analysis.

Five for source integration. Sources from your preliminary bibliography appear in the deck and video, alongside additional sources you have located in your fuller research between the proposal-and-bibliography checkpoint and the draft. Five or more sources should be visible in the project itself, named in the prose where appropriate, not just listed at the end.

Five for technical execution. The deck opens. The video plays. Audio is audible. Embedded media works.

What happens after you submit

I will give you written feedback on your draft within one week. The feedback will be the most detailed feedback I give in the course; use it for your final revision.

You will also receive structured feedback from two of your classmates as part of the Peer Review checkpoint due July 26. You will give peer review on two of their drafts in turn. Between my feedback and your peer feedback, you will have plenty of material to work with for the final.

If you submit something you know is incomplete

Email me. Submit what you have. A 14-slide draft with a real argument is more useful to revise than a 20-slide draft of placeholder text. A draft that misses a section can be fixed; a final submission that misses a section cannot.

AI use

Allowed with disclosure. If you used AI for any part of the draft, include a brief acknowledgment on the sources slide naming the tool and what you used it for.
